I recently read [this post](https://geohot.github.io/blog/jekyll/update/2024/12/01/the-soul.html)&mdash;while I think extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, it got me thinking beyond the human soul.

**What makes something feel *alive*?**

---
The answer is uncertainty the more you think about it.

Why are people scared of small bugs? Some find them disgusting and this is a (good) biological response.
But the majority of people who are scared of bugs are scared because they are unpredictable.
You don't know where or how the bug will [move](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle), and that's what makes it scary.
You have some sense, but there is still *uncertainty*.

On the flip side, I've never met anyone who are scared of small rocks, there is no inherent uncertainty with them; they are predictable.

---

This applies to us humans in scale as well.

If you think about what makes a human feel like a {zombie, NPC, robot, etc.}, the common factor between these are that they feel predictable to a certain degree.

If an NPC starts to deviate from how they usually (should) behave, you would consider them more *alive*

---

What makes something feel alive is a spectrum, a truly [chaotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory) system does not feel alive either.

You can't sense any patterns or familiarity, it is beyond our (current) comprehension.

---

The sense of something feeling alive is a byproduct of a [certain amount of uncertainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(information_theory)) in the system.
