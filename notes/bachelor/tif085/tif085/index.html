Thermodynamics and Theory of Heat

Thermal Efficiency

Let’s start with something which is quite simple to understand. The famous quote that,

“Energy is neither created nor destroyed, only changed from one form to another”.

With this in mind we understand that we never ‘create’ energy. It’s also a sensible conclusion that if we had machines with 100% efficiency, we wouldn’t have whole fields in physics about energy-efficiency.

Informal definition of thermal efficiency

(We will later define a more formal definition of thermal efficiency)

Thermal expansion

Imagine we have a block of some kind of material. If we apply heat to this block it’s natural that it will expand. The same logic goes if we lower the heat/temperature - it will compress instead.

Formula for Thermal Expansion

L=L0(1+αΔT),L = L_0 (1 + \alpha \Delta T),L=L0​(1+αΔT),

where α\alphaα is called the expansion coefficient with unit(s) [K−1][K^{-1}][K−1].

This is for a 1D expansion, if the expansion happens in 2D it is,

L=L0(1+βΔT)L = L_0 (1 + \beta \Delta T)L=L0​(1+βΔT)

where β\betaβ is the expansion coefficient for 2D expansion. Note, β≈2α\beta \approx 2\alphaβ≈2α is only true for most metals, not all materials.

Thermal Conductivity for Simple Geometric Objects

Say if we have a system of 2 bodies, one which gains energy/heats up from an external source, with an opening to the second body.

This will transfer the heat from the hot body ThT_hTh​ to the colder body TcT_cTc​ since some heat will be lost in the transfer. The opening has a so-called thermal conductivity, the ability to transfer heat. With this in mind we can derive,

P=dQdt=AkTh−Tcl,P = \frac{dQ}{dt} = Ak \frac{T_h - T_c}{l},P=dtdQ​=AklTh​−Tc​​,

where,

P−Effect [W]Q−Heat exchange [J]A−Cross-sectional Areak−Thermal Conductivity [WmK]l−Length of the opening\begin{align*} P & - \text{Effect } [W] \newline Q & - \text{Heat exchange } [J] \newline A & - \text{Cross-sectional Area} \newline k & - \text{Thermal Conductivity } [\frac{W}{mK}] \newline l & - \text{Length of the opening} \end{align*}PQAkl​−Effect [W]−Heat exchange [J]−Cross-sectional Area−Thermal Conductivity [mKW​]−Length of the opening​

We can rewrite this also as,

P=−Ak dTdx,P = -Ak \ \frac{dT}{dx},P=−Ak dxdT​,

or more generally,

P=−Ak ∇TP = -Ak \ \nabla TP=−Ak ∇T

Thermal Conductivity for more Complex Geometric Objects

For more complex geometric shapes, we need to consider how the thickness of the body increases/decreases. For example, for a cylinder, the formula becomes,

P=−A⏟2πlrk dTdr=2πlr⋅k dTdr.P = -\underbrace{A}_{2\pi lr} k \ \frac{dT}{dr} = 2\pi lr \cdot k \ \frac{dT}{dr}.P=−2πlrA​​k drdT​=2πlr⋅k drdT​.

If we’re looking at a cylinder that generates heats in its core for example, being near the core (small inner radius) will obviously be warmer than being at its surface (larger inner radius). Since 2πl2\pi l2πl is always constant – we can rewrite the equation and integrate both sides to get,

∫ThTc−2πl ⋅kP=∫R1R2drr⇒P=2πklln⁡(R2R1)(Th−Tc),\int_{T_h}^{T_c} -\frac{2\pi l\ \cdot k}{P} = \int_{R_1}^{R_2} \frac{dr}{r} \Rightarrow P = \frac{2\pi k l}{\ln(\frac{R_2}{R_1})} (T_h - T_c),∫Th​Tc​​−P2πl ⋅k​=∫R1​R2​​rdr​⇒P=ln(R1​R2​​)2πkl​(Th​−Tc​),

where R1R_1R1​ and R2R_2R2​ are two inner-radii to the cylinder.

Suppose we have a system which upholds a certain temperature TiT_iTi​ with a constant effect of PPP – Now consider if we cut the constant effect of heat, PPP and let the system naturally cool down due to its surrounding temperature being TcT_cTc​.

We can describe this relation with the following,

dQ=−cm dT,dQ = -cm \ dT,dQ=−cm dT,

where ccc is the conductivity coefficient and mmm is the mass of the substance.

With a bit of rewriting and integrating we also get,

What Thermal Conductivity really means

Just so we understand the equation Q=cmΔTQ = cm \Delta TQ=cmΔT – the ccc coefficient tells us how much energy we need to put into a substance to change its temperature. For example, ≈4180J\approx 4180 J≈4180J are required to increase the temperature of 1 kg of water by 1 ‘kelvin’. Often we can use ∘^\circ∘C as well - but this is due to the difference between two temperature being absolute. So really it’s an increase in any kind of temperature, just by 1 index.

Latent Heat

Last thing we’re about to cover is the so-called Latent heat release of required to transition from one phase to another.

The formula for how much energy is needed is,

Q=mLQ = mLQ=mL

Where LLL is the specific latent heat for a particular substance with units [J/kg][J/kg][J/kg].
