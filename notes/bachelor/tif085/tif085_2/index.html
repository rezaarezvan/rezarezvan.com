Ideal gases

An ideal gas is a gas that is not subject to interparticle interactions. Also, commonly said to not being subject to react with other substances.

In many applications of thermodynamics, ideal gases often come up, so it’s natural to understand them within a thermodynamic perspective.

Gases and Pressure

Before we begin with the real thermodynamical of gases we need a bit of background with gases. Boyle’s law gives us a mathematical formula for our intuition of gases. Say we have set up an experiment where the mass of a gas and the temperature is constant. The only thing that could vary now is the pressure within in the gas, and its volume. How we define pressure is the key here - pressure is, in non-science terms, the force applied per area unit. Usually we write pressure as, PPP, with the formula:

P=FAP = \frac{F}{A}P=AF​

So if we expand our container which the gas is held in, intuitively, the pressure should release/decrease. This is exactly what Boyle’s law tells us.

Boyle’s law

PV=ConstantPV = \text{Constant}PV=Constant

This means we can, for example, write,

P1V1=P2V2P_1 V_1 = P_2 V_2P1​V1​=P2​V2​

But in many cases we can’t keep the amount of particles (mass) and temperature constant - so how do we describe it then?

Ideal Gas Law

Suppose we have a cylinder with height hhh, and a surface area of AAA, containing some gas. Simplified, the gas will have a gravitational force FMgF_{Mg}FMg​, since the gas has a mass MMM.

We can therefore describe the pressure as,

M=ρAhF=MgP=FA=ρAhgA=ρhg\begin{align*} M & = \rho A h \newline F & = Mg \newline P & = \frac{F}{A} = \frac{\rho \cancel{A} h g}{\cancel{A}} = \rho h g \end{align*}MFP​=ρAh=Mg=AF​=A​ρA​hg​=ρhg​

Kinetic Gas Theory

Our day to day definition of temperature is how ‘hot’ or ‘cold’ it is - but what is temperature really describing? One can think that temperature describes the average kinetic energy in a object. This of course varies with what substance we have and on many other factors - but in general we can say that gas particle, has an average kinetic energy of:

Eaverage=α2kBT, where α is so called ’degrees of freedom’E_{average} = \frac{\alpha}{2} k_B T \text{, where } \alpha \text{ is so called 'degrees of freedom'}Eaverage​=2α​kB​T, where α is so called ’degrees of freedom’

Therefore the total energy in a gas is:

Etotal=NEaverage=Nα2kBTE_{total} = N E_{average} = N \frac{\alpha}{2} k_B TEtotal​=NEaverage​=N2α​kB​T

In our course and its applications, where we will only encounter monoatomic and diatomic gases - α=3\alpha = 3α=3 for monoatomic and α=5\alpha = 5α=5 diatomic.

There’s also a so called ‘inner energy’ in the gas which we denote with UUU - for a monoatomic gas, UUU is generally:

U=n32RTU = n \frac{3}{2}RTU=n23​RT

Conductivity in Ideal Gases

Often when we encounter gases in a thermal system - we will encounter two different categories of them. One where we have constant volume, the gas can not expand more than its initial state. One where we have constant pressure, we may have a lid that can be expanded, therefore the volume will change.

For these kinds of problems the usual Q=cmΔTQ = cm\Delta TQ=cmΔT becomes tricky. But luckily, the formula for these are:

Q=nCvΔTQ=nCpΔTQ = n C_v \Delta T \newline Q = n C_p \Delta TQ=nCv​ΔTQ=nCp​ΔT

Where the subscript of the CCC denotes which is constant and the other varying.

With this we can also get formulas for CvC_vCv​ and CpC_pCp​ for ideal gases:

Cv=α2RCp=Cv+RC_v = \frac{\alpha}{2}R \newline C_p = C_v + RCv​=2α​RCp​=Cv​+R

Work by Gases

Now that we’ve encountered the different kind of categories of problems - we can understand which kind can perform work and which can not. If we have constant volume it means the gas cannot expand or ‘push’ its surrounding. Therefore it cant do work!

But in the case where we have constant pressure, but not volume, the gas can perform work. As we remember from mechanics - work is defined as:

dW=F⃗dxdW = \vec{F} dxdW=Fdx

Since we have another equation for the force, F=PAF = PAF=PA, we can write:

dW=P(Adx)=PdVdW = P(A dx) = P dVdW=P(Adx)=PdV

We usually write this as

W=∫ifPdVW = \int_i^f P dVW=∫if​PdV

Now the ‘direction’ is really important here - and one may wonder what ‘direction’ do we have when we’re dealing with thermodynamics? Direction within thermodynamics means if the gas expands (W>0)(W > 0)(W>0) or if it compresses (W<0)(W < 0)(W<0).

Phase and Transition variables

In a given system, we may have a heat flux, QQQ - we have a ‘inner energy’, UUU, and the system may do work, WWW. Notice how I used may for QQQ and WWW - this is because they are so called ‘phase variables’, this because they are only present during a ‘phase’ or ‘process’.

While the system always has a inner energy, UUU.

Thermodynamics

Now we have finally arrived at what this section of the course is all about - ‘Thermodynamics’. So how would one describe thermodynamics? Our lecturer described it as, “A transaction system for energy”. Which I find as a simple - yet good - explanation. It really is about how systems ‘handle’ energy in and out of it.

We’ve already defined a lot of variables that are essential in thermodynamics:

Q - heat exchange with the systems environment

U - the systems inner energy

W - Work which the gas performs - here we have to careful, sometimes work is defined by the work done by the outside on the system.

0th Law of Thermodynamics

It’s quite a funny name since it’s a physics law - and not a Computer Science one - that it’s 0 indexed. But this law came after all the other laws, because people just assumed this was the case - but a proper formal law was needed.

The 0th law of thermodynamics states that:

“Suppose we have 3 bodies, 1, 2, and, 3. In that order, if 1 and 2 are in thermal equilibrium - 2 and 3 are also in thermal equilibrium. Then 1 and 3 are also in thermal equilibrium”.

As stated - a quite ‘obvious’ law that is not so powerful, but needed.

1th Law of Thermodynamics

Now this is a really powerful law - The 1th law of thermodynamics states that:

Q=ΔU+WQ = \Delta U + WQ=ΔU+W

If we ‘talk’ through this forumla we understand that - the heat flux/heat exchange in a system, will be the difference in inner energy added with the work done by the gas in the system.

Isoprocesses

As we already touched upon - thermodynamical gas problems often come in two categories. Here we will formally define them. The prefix ‘iso’ means that something is unchanged, so therefore a isobar process means that the pressure is constant. In all of these different processes, the ideal gas law and the difference in inner energy will be the same for all.

PV=nRTΔU=nαRΔTPV = nRT \newline \Delta U = n \frac{\alpha}{R} \Delta TPV=nRTΔU=nRα​ΔT

Isobar process

In a isobar process, the pressure will be held constant throughout the system. Which means:

Wif=P(Vf−Vi)Q=nCpΔTΔU=nCvΔTW_{if} = P(V_f - V_i) \newline Q = n C_p \Delta T \newline \Delta U = n C_v \Delta TWif​=P(Vf​−Vi​)Q=nCp​ΔTΔU=nCv​ΔT

Isochoric process

In a isochoric process, the volume will instead be held constant throughout the system. Which leads to:

Wif=0Q=nCvΔTΔU=nCvΔTW_{if} = 0 \newline Q = n C_v \Delta T \newline \Delta U = n C_v \Delta TWif​=0Q=nCv​ΔTΔU=nCv​ΔT

Isothermal process

In a isothermal process, the temperature will instead be held constant throughout the system. Which means:

Wif=∫PdV=nRT∗ln(VfVi)ΔU=nCvΔT=0Q=+WW_{if} = \int P dV = nRT * ln(\frac{V_f}{V_i}) \newline \Delta U = n C_v \Delta T = 0 \newline Q = +WWif​=∫PdV=nRT∗ln(Vi​Vf​​)ΔU=nCv​ΔT=0Q=+W

Adiabatic process

In a adiabatic process, the heat exchange is 0 - this is quite a special case, but this means:

Q=0W=nCv(Ti−Tf)W=−nCVΔTΔU=nCvΔTQ = 0 \newline W = n C_v (T_i - T_f) \newline W = - n C_V \Delta T \newline \Delta U = n C_v \Delta TQ=0W=nCv​(Ti​−Tf​)W=−nCV​ΔTΔU=nCv​ΔT

With this we can see that ΔU\Delta UΔU has to ‘pay the price’ since Q=0Q = 0Q=0. This means the system takes its own inner energy to complete the transaction in energy.

We also have one more formula in the adiabatic case:

PVγ=ConstantPV^\gamma = ConstantPVγ=Constant

With the ideal gas law we can find that:

TVγ−1=ConstantTV^{\gamma - 1} = ConstantTVγ−1=Constant

As well as:

TP1−γγ=ConstantTP^{\frac{1 - \gamma}{\gamma}} = ConstantTPγ1−γ​=Constant
