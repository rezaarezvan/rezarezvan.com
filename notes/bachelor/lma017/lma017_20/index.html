Introduction

In this part we’ll cover surface integrals over vector fields as well as two very important theorems.

Surface integrals over vector fields

Let SSS be a parametric surface, parameterized by, r⃗:D→R2\vec{r}: D \rarr \mathbb{R}^2r:D→R2 and F:R3→R3F: \mathbb{R}^3 \rarr \mathbb{R}^3F:R3→R3, be a vector field. Then:

∬SF⃗⋅dS=∬DF⃗(r⃗(u,v))⋅ru⃗×rv⃗ du dv\iint_S \vec{F} \cdot dS = \iint_D \vec{F}(\vec{r}(u, v)) \cdot \vec{r_u} \times \vec{r_v}\ du\ dv∬S​F⋅dS=∬D​F(r(u,v))⋅ru​​×rv​​ du dv

We call this the flux of F⃗\vec{F}F through SSS.

Example

Given, F⃗=⟨z,y,x⟩\vec{F} = \langle z, y, x \rangleF=⟨z,y,x⟩, find the flux of this vector field across the unit sphere.

So, we have the surface:

S={(x,y,z)∣x2+y2+z2≤1}S = \{(x, y, z) | x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq 1 \}S={(x,y,z)∣x2+y2+z2≤1}

As always, good idea to change into polar coordinates, so a good parameterization is:

r⃗(ϕ,θ)=⟨sin(ϕ) cos(θ),sin(ϕ) sin(θ),cos(ϕ)⟩ ∣ 0≤ϕ≤π,0≤θ≤2π\vec{r}(\phi, \theta) = \langle sin(\phi)\ cos(\theta), sin(\phi)\ sin(\theta), cos(\phi) \rangle \ | \ 0 \leq \phi \leq \pi, 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pir(ϕ,θ)=⟨sin(ϕ) cos(θ),sin(ϕ) sin(θ),cos(ϕ)⟩ ∣ 0≤ϕ≤π,0≤θ≤2π

NB: This computation is just tedious (and we’ll see how we can reduce this shortly)

ru⃗×rv⃗=⟨sin2(ϕ) cos(θ),sin2(ϕ) sin(θ),sin(ϕ) cos(ϕ)⟩\vec{r_u} \times \vec{r_v} = \langle sin^2(\phi)\ cos(\theta), sin^2(\phi)\ sin(\theta), sin(\phi)\ cos(\phi) \rangleru​​×rv​​=⟨sin2(ϕ) cos(θ),sin2(ϕ) sin(θ),sin(ϕ) cos(ϕ)⟩

Now, one can solve this integral by taking the dot product and applying our definition:

∫02π∫0π⟨cos(ϕ),sin(ϕ) sin(θ),sin(ϕ) cos(θ)⟩⋅⟨sin2(ϕ) cos(θ),sin2(ϕ) sin(θ),sin(ϕ) cos(ϕ)⟩\int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi} \langle cos(\phi), sin(\phi)\ sin(\theta), sin(\phi)\ cos(\theta) \rangle \cdot \langle sin^2(\phi)\ cos(\theta), sin^2(\phi)\ sin(\theta), sin(\phi)\ cos(\phi) \rangle∫02π​∫0π​⟨cos(ϕ),sin(ϕ) sin(θ),sin(ϕ) cos(θ)⟩⋅⟨sin2(ϕ) cos(θ),sin2(ϕ) sin(θ),sin(ϕ) cos(ϕ)⟩

Let’s see now how we can do the same example, with fewer computations, first some theory.

Orientation

For surfaces, we say that positive orientation is “pointing outwards”. For hollow surfaces this is always the case. If our parameterization is given by a single function, then we always have the correct choice.

However, imagine half the unit sphere with a filled base. The base with parameterization: D={(r,θ)∣0≤r≤1,0≤θ≤2π}D = \{(r, \theta) | 0 \leq r \leq 1, 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi \}D={(r,θ)∣0≤r≤1,0≤θ≤2π}. Is going to be a negative integral.

The Divergence Theorem

For a vector field, F⃗:R3→R3\vec{F}: \mathbb{R}^3 \rarr \mathbb{R}^3F:R3→R3, the divergence is:

div F⃗=∂P∂x+∂Q∂y+∂R∂zdiv\ \vec{F} = \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \dfrac{\partial R}{\partial z}div F=∂x∂P​+∂y∂Q​+∂z∂R​

Let SSS be a hollow surface and let EEE be the 3-dimensional region it surrounds (∂E=S\partial E = S∂E=S).

Then:

∬SF⃗⋅dS=∭Ediv F⃗ dV\iint_S \vec{F} \cdot dS = \iiint_E div\ \vec{F}\ dV∬S​F⋅dS=∭E​div F dV

Equivalently:

∬∂EF⃗⋅dS=∭Ediv F⃗ dV\iint_{\partial E} \vec{F} \cdot dS = \iiint_E div\ \vec{F}\ dV∬∂E​F⋅dS=∭E​div F dV

Now let’s take the sample example.

Example

Find the flux of F⃗=⟨z,y,x\vec{F} = \langle z, y, xF=⟨z,y,x on the unit sphere.

div F⃗=∂P∂x+∂Q∂y+∂R∂z=0+0+1=1div\ \vec{F} = \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \dfrac{\partial R}{\partial z} = 0 + 0 + 1 = 1div F=∂x∂P​+∂y∂Q​+∂z∂R​=0+0+1=1 ∬SF⃗⋅dS=∭E1dV\iint_S \vec{F} \cdot dS = \iiint_E 1 dV∬S​F⋅dS=∭E​1dV

We could now just transform to polar coordinates, but, this is the volume of the unit sphere, which is just:

4π3\boxed{\dfrac{4\pi}{3}}34π​​

Stokes’ Theorem

So far we have seen Green’s Theorem as well as The Divergence theorem. But what is the reason that Green’s Theorem only works in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3?

Well, Stokes Theorem is essentially Green’s Theorem but in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3.

For F⃗:R3→R3\vec{F}: \mathbb{R}^3 \rarr \mathbb{R}^3F:R3→R3, we define its curl as:

NB: it is also called rot and sometimes denoted as ∇×F⃗\nabla \times \vec{F}∇×F.

curl F⃗=⟨∂R∂y−∂Q∂z,∂P∂z−∂R∂x,∂Q∂x−∂P∂y⟩curl\ \vec{F} = \langle \dfrac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial z}, \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial z} - \dfrac{\partial R}{\partial x}, \dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y} \ranglecurl F=⟨∂y∂R​−∂z∂Q​,∂z∂P​−∂x∂R​,∂x∂Q​−∂y∂P​⟩

If S⊂R3S \subset \mathbb{R}^3S⊂R3 is a surface, surrounded by a simple, closed curve in R3\mathbb{R}^3R3. Then:

∫CF⃗⋅dr=∬Scurl F dS\int_C \vec{F} \cdot dr = \iint_S curl\ F\ dS∫C​F⋅dr=∬S​curl F dS

Example

Compute ∫CF⋅dr\int_C F \cdot dr∫C​F⋅dr for F⃗=⟨−y2,x,z2⟩\vec{F} = \langle -y^2, x, z^2 \rangleF=⟨−y2,x,z2⟩ and CCC being the intersection of y+z=2y + z = 2y+z=2 with x2+y1=1x^2 + y^1 = 1x2+y1=1.

Let’s find the curl:

curl F⃗=⟨0,0,1+2y⟩curl\ \vec{F} = \langle 0, 0, 1 + 2y \ranglecurl F=⟨0,0,1+2y⟩

We need to find the surface, the intersection of y+z=2y + z = 2y+z=2 and x2+y2=1x^2 + y^2 = 1x2+y2=1 will just be the unit disk but at a slight tilt.

∬Scurl F⃗ dS\iint_S curl\ \vec{F}\ dS∬S​curl F dS

Is just the flux through this region, let’s express this in polar coordinates

D={(r,θ)∣0≤r≤1,0≤θ≤2π}D = \{(r, \theta) | 0 \leq r \leq 1, 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi \}D={(r,θ)∣0≤r≤1,0≤θ≤2π}

Meaning:

∬Scurl F⃗ dS=∬Dcurl F⃗⋅ru⃗×rv⃗ dA\iint_S curl\ \vec{F}\ dS = \iint_D curl\ \vec{F} \cdot \vec{r_u} \times \vec{r_v}\ dA∬S​curl F dS=∬D​curl F⋅ru​​×rv​​ dA

Let’s find a parameterization of the surface:

r⃗(u,v)=⟨u,v,2−v⟩\vec{r}(u, v) = \langle u, v, 2 - v \rangler(u,v)=⟨u,v,2−v⟩ ru⃗(u,v)=⟨1,0,0⟩\vec{r_u}(u, v) = \langle 1, 0, 0 \rangleru​​(u,v)=⟨1,0,0⟩ rv⃗(u,v)=⟨0,1,−1⟩\vec{r_v}(u, v) = \langle 0, 1, -1 \ranglerv​​(u,v)=⟨0,1,−1⟩ ru⃗×rv⃗=⟨0,1,1⟩\vec{r_u} \times \vec{r_v} = \langle 0, 1, 1 \rangleru​​×rv​​=⟨0,1,1⟩ ∬Dcurl F⃗⋅ru⃗×rv⃗ dA=∬D⟨0,0,1+2y⟩⋅⟨0,1,1⟩ dA\iint_D curl\ \vec{F} \cdot \vec{r_u} \times \vec{r_v}\ dA = \iint_D \langle 0, 0, 1 + 2y \rangle \cdot \langle 0, 1, 1 \rangle\ dA∬D​curl F⋅ru​​×rv​​ dA=∬D​⟨0,0,1+2y⟩⋅⟨0,1,1⟩ dA ∬D1+2y dA\iint_D 1 + 2y\ dA∬D​1+2y dA ∫02π∫01(1+2rsin(θ))r dr dθ\int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^1 (1 + 2r sin(\theta))r\ dr\ d\theta∫02π​∫01​(1+2rsin(θ))r dr dθ ∫02π∫01r+2r2sin(θ) dr dθ\int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^1 r + 2r^2 sin(\theta)\ dr\ d\theta∫02π​∫01​r+2r2sin(θ) dr dθ ∫01∫02πr+2r2sin(θ) dθ dr\int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} r + 2r^2 sin(\theta)\ d\theta\ dr∫01​∫02π​r+2r2sin(θ) dθ dr

sin(θ)sin(\theta)sin(θ) has symmetry around 2π2\pi2π, therefore the integral of that is 000.

Compute ∬Scurl F⃗ dS\iint_S curl\ \vec{F}\ dS∬S​curl F dS where F⃗=⟨xz,yz,xy⟩\vec{F} = \langle xz, yz, xy \rangleF=⟨xz,yz,xy⟩ and SSS is the part of the sphere of radius 2 around (0,0,0)(0, 0, 0)(0,0,0) that lies inside the cylinder x2+y2≤1x^2 + y^2 \leq 1x2+y2≤1 and above the xyxyxy-plane.

{x2+y2=1x2+y2+z2=4\begin{cases} x^2 + y^2 = 1 \newline x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 4 \newline \end{cases}{x2+y2=1x2+y2+z2=4​
