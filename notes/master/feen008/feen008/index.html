Introduction

Most SDEs take the form,

dx(t)=f(x(t),t) dt⏟drift+L(x(t),t) dβ(t)⏟diffusion,\begin{equation} dx(t) = \underbrace{f(x(t), t) \ dt}_{\text{drift}} + \underbrace{L(x(t), t) \ d\beta(t)}_{\text{diffusion}}, \end{equation}dx(t)=driftf(x(t),t) dt​​+diffusionL(x(t),t) dβ(t)​​,​​

where x(t)x(t)x(t) is a stochastic process and β(t)\beta(t)β(t) is Brownian motion 1.

The SDE in Equation (1) is shorthand for the (Itô, teaser!) integral equation,

x(t)−x(t0)=∫t0tf(x(s),s) ds+∫t0tL(x(s),s) dβ(s).x(t) - x(t_0) = \int_{t_0}^{t} f(x(s), s) \ ds + \int_{t_0}^{t} L(x(s), s) \ d\beta(s).x(t)−x(t0​)=∫t0​t​f(x(s),s) ds+∫t0​t​L(x(s),s) dβ(s).

Suppose f(x,t)=2,L(x,t)=0f(x, t) = 2, L(x, t) = 0f(x,t)=2,L(x,t)=0, ⇒dxdt=2\Rightarrow \frac{dx}{dt} = 2⇒dtdx​=2.

We also assume that x(0)∼N(0,2)x(0) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2)x(0)∼N(0,2). See Figure 1.

  2. Increments are independent unless intervals overlap.

Suppose f(x,t)=0,L(x,t)=2f(x, t) = 0, L(x, t) = 2f(x,t)=0,L(x,t)=2, ⇒dx(t)=2 dβ(t)\Rightarrow dx(t) = 2 \ d \beta(t)⇒dx(t)=2 dβ(t).

We also assume that x(0)∼N(0,1)x(0) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)x(0)∼N(0,1). See Figure 2.

Motivation and Dynamic Models

SDEs are commonly used to model object dynamics (cars, airplanes, etc).

Often, time-discretized versions are used for filtering, smoothing, and prediction.

Constant Velocity Models (Scalar Example)

Suppose x(t)=[x(t)v(t)]T\mathbf{x}(t) = \begin{bmatrix} x(t) & v(t) \end{bmatrix}^Tx(t)=[x(t)​v(t)​]T represents the position and velocity of an object.

According to the constant velocity model,

dx(t)=[0100]x(t) dt+[0q]dβ(t),⇔=[v(t) dt0]+[0q dβ(t)].\begin{align*} d \mathbf{x}(t) & = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \newline 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}(t) \ dt + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \newline q \end{bmatrix} d \beta(t), \newline \Leftrightarrow & = \begin{bmatrix} v(t) \ dt \newline 0 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \newline q \ d \beta(t) \end{bmatrix}. \end{align*}dx(t)⇔​=[00​10​]x(t) dt+[0q​]dβ(t),=[v(t) dt0​]+[0q dβ(t)​].​

The position follows the velocity, whereas the velocity is a Brownian motion.

The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process 2,

dv(t)=−θv(t) dt+σ dβ(t),dv(t) = -\theta v(t) \ dt + \sigma \ d \beta(t),dv(t)=−θv(t) dt+σ dβ(t),

is used in several fields, e.g., in physics to model particles moving with friction.

The Black-Scholes equation 3 is widely used in finacne to model the value of “financial instruments”.

A basic version of this equation is,

d s(t)=μs(t) dt+σs(t) dβ(t),d \ s(t) = \mu s(t) \ dt + \sigma s(t) \ d \beta(t),d s(t)=μs(t) dt+σs(t) dβ(t),

where s(t)s(t)s(t) is the stock price.

The stochastic Lotka-Volterra 4 model describes the predator-prey dynamics,

dx(t)=x(t)(a−by(t)) dt+σ1x(t) dβ1(t),dy(t)=y(t)(−c+dx(t)) dt+σ2y(t) dβ2(t).\begin{align*} dx(t) & = x(t)(a - b y(t)) \ dt + \sigma_1 x(t) \ d \beta_1(t), \newline dy(t) & = y(t)(-c + d x(t)) \ dt + \sigma_2 y(t) \ d \beta_2(t). \end{align*}dx(t)dy(t)​=x(t)(a−by(t)) dt+σ1​x(t) dβ1​(t),=y(t)(−c+dx(t)) dt+σ2​y(t) dβ2​(t).​

Here, x(t)x(t)x(t) and y(t)y(t)y(t) are the prey and the predator populations, respectively, and β1(t)\beta_1(t)β1​(t) and β2(t)\beta_2(t)β2​(t) are independent Brownian motions.

Diffusion can be used as a (deep) generative model as well (and this is what we are mostly interested in).

The basic idea is,

  • Recursively remove structure from data (make it noisier!).
  • Train network to remove noise and obtain structure again.
  • Generate (new) data by feeding noise to the trained network.

The forward process is generally an SDE,

dx(t)=f(x,t) dt+L(t) dβ(t).d x(t) = f(x, t) \ dt + L(t) \ d \beta(t).dx(t)=f(x,t) dt+L(t) dβ(t).

We are going to get a solid understanding of theoretical results used to train and perform inference in these models.

Image generation is arguably the most well-known application. A few other examples are,

  • Besides bridging noise and data distributions we can bridge, e.g., low- and high-resolution images.
  • Molecular and drug design, e.g., protein structures.
  • Sequential data, e.g., human pose sequences and financial data.
  • Motion planning and reinforcement learning with diffusion policies.

The Engineering Approach: White Noise

Recall that we study SDEs on the form,

dx(t)=f(x(t),t) dt+L(x(t),t) dβ(t),x(t)−x(t0)=∫t0tf(x(s),s) ds+∫t0tL(x(s),s) dβ(s).\begin{align*} dx(t) & = f(x(t), t) \ dt + L(x(t), t) \ d \beta(t), \newline x(t) - x(t_0) & = \int_{t_0}^t f(x(s), s) \ ds + \int_{t_0}^t L(x(s), s) \ d \beta(s). \end{align*}dx(t)x(t)−x(t0​)​=f(x(t),t) dt+L(x(t),t) dβ(t),=∫t0​t​f(x(s),s) ds+∫t0​t​L(x(s),s) dβ(s).​

In engineering literature, the notation dβ(t)d \beta(t)dβ(t) is uncommon in integrals.

Imagine for a second that if β(t)\beta(t)β(t) were differentiable (it isn’t!), we could introduce w(t)=dβ(t)dtw(t) = \frac{d \beta(t)}{dt}w(t)=dtdβ(t)​,

dx(t)dt=f(x,t)+L(x,t) w(t),x(t1)−x(t0)=∫t0t1f(x(s),s) ds+∫t0t1L(x(s),s)w(s) ds,\begin{align*} \frac{dx(t)}{dt} & = f(x, t) + L(x, t) \ w(t), \newline x(t_1) - x(t_0) & = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} f(x(s), s) \ ds + \int_{t_0}^{t_1} L(x(s), s) w(s) \ ds, \end{align*}dtdx(t)​x(t1​)−x(t0​)​=f(x,t)+L(x,t) w(t),=∫t0​t1​​f(x(s),s) ds+∫t0​t1​​L(x(s),s)w(s) ds,​

where w(t)w(t)w(t) is white noise.

In engineering literature, this is the standard form to express SDEs.

However, what is white noise? We know that β(t)\beta(t)β(t) has independent increments.

It is common to assume that w(t)w(t)w(t) is a Gaussian process with,

E[w(t)]=0,Cov(w(t),w(t+τ))=δ(τ).\begin{align*} \mathbb{E}[w(t)] & = 0, \newline \mathrm{Cov}(w(t), w(t + \tau)) & = \delta(\tau). \end{align*}E[w(t)]Cov(w(t),w(t+τ))​=0,=δ(τ).​

However, if we naively use these assumptions to derive, e.g., E[X(t)]\mathbb{E}[X(t)]E[X(t)] and Cov(x(t))\mathrm{Cov}(x(t))Cov(x(t)), we get the wrong expression for Cov(x(t))\mathrm{Cov}(x(t))Cov(x(t)).

“The white Gaussian process is mathematical fiction”. - AH Jazwinski.

We’ll take the (more) mathematical approach and express SDEs using dβ(t)d \beta(t)dβ(t) and learn about Itô calculus 5 to enable us to derive the correct expressions.

We’ll occasionally use the notation x(ω)x(\omega)x(ω), but generally characterize random variables using,

Introduction to Stochastic Processes

SDEs describe a class of stochastic processes (SDEs cannot describe all stochastic processes).

Definition 2 (Definition: Stochastic process)

A stochastic process is a family of (jointly distributed) random variables {x(t)}t∈T\{x(t)\}_{t \in T}{x(t)}t∈T​.

Processes described by SDEs have continuous states xxx and parameters ttt.

For a given time, ttt, we have stochastic variables, x(t)x(t)x(t). Note that we do not separate the notation for the stochastic/random variable and its realization.

Often, we also use a shorthand for its density, p(x,t)=p(x(t))p(x, t) = p(x(t))p(x,t)=p(x(t)) and allow our notation for the argument to specify the density that we evaluate.

How is convergence defined?

How should one define convergence for a sequence of random variables?

A few (decently) intuitive ways are,

Summary

Most SDEs take the form,

dx(t)=f(x(t),t) dt⏟drift+L(x(t),t) dβ(t)⏟diffusion,\begin{equation} dx(t) = \underbrace{f(x(t), t) \ dt}_{\text{drift}} + \underbrace{L(x(t), t) \ d\beta(t)}_{\text{diffusion}}, \end{equation}dx(t)=driftf(x(t),t) dt​​+diffusionL(x(t),t) dβ(t)​​,​​

where x(t)x(t)x(t) is a stochastic process and β(t)\beta(t)β(t) is a Brownian motion.

SDEs describe a class of stochastic processes useful in many areas (economics, physics, etc). Where we specifically (will) focus on (deep) generative models.

We can use mean square convergence to define convergence, derivates, and Riemann integrals for stochastic processes.

Equation (3) is shorthand for,

x(t)−x(t0)=∫t0tf(x(s),s) ds+∫t0tL(x(s),s) dβ(s).x(t) - x(t_0) = \int_{t_0}^t f(x(s), s) \ ds + \int_{t_0}^t L(x(s), s) \ d\beta(s).x(t)−x(t0​)=∫t0​t​f(x(s),s) ds+∫t0​t​L(x(s),s) dβ(s).

Note, we can use mean square Riemann integrals to define ∫t0tf(x(s),s) ds\int_{t_0}^{t} f(x(s), s) \ ds∫t0​t​f(x(s),s) ds but not ∫t0tL(x(s),s) dβ(s)\int_{t_0}^{t} L(x(s), s) \ d\beta(s)∫t0​t​L(x(s),s) dβ(s).

We need Itô calc for that ;).
