Introduction

In this part we will cover duality for Linear Programs (LPs) and the corresponding weak & strong duality theorems. Further, we will also discuss sensitivity analysis and complementarity of (dual) LPs, which will give us an alternative view of the simplex method.a

Primal-Dual LP Problems

Definition 1 (Primal LP Problem)

Consider the primal LP problem on standard form,

(P)z⋆={min⁡ cTxsubject to Ax=b x≥0\begin{align*} (P) \quad & \newline z^{\star} & = \begin{cases} \min \ & \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \newline \text{subject to} \ & A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \newline \ & \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} \end{cases} \end{align*}(P)z⋆​=⎩⎨⎧​min subject to  ​cTxAx=bx≥0​​

where z⋆z^{\star}z⋆ is the optimal objective value.

Definition 2 (Dual LP Problem)

The dual LP problem corresponding to the primal LP problem (P)(P)(P) is,

(D)q⋆={max⁡ bTysubject to ATy≤c\begin{align*} (D) \quad & \newline q^{\star} & = \begin{cases} \max \ & \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y} \newline \text{subject to} \ & A^T \mathbf{y} \leq \mathbf{c} \end{cases} \end{align*}(D)q⋆​={max subject to ​bTyATy≤c​​

where q⋆q^{\star}q⋆ is the optimal objective value.

Explanation (Derivation of the Dual Problem)

We know that the Lagrangian for the primal LP problem is,

L(x,y)=cTx−yT(b−Ax)=bTy+xT(ATy−c)\begin{align*} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) & = \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}^T (\mathbf{b} - A \mathbf{x}) \newline & = \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y} + \mathbf{x}^T (A^T \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{c}) \end{align*}L(x,y)​=cTx−yT(b−Ax)=bTy+xT(ATy−c)​

thus, the dual function q(y)q(\mathbf{y})q(y) is,

q(y)=inf⁡ bTy+xT(ATy−c)subject to x≥0={bTy,ATy−c≤0−∞,otherwise\begin{align*} \begin{align*} q(\mathbf{y}) = & \inf \ \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y} + \mathbf{x}^T (A^T \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{c}) \newline & \text{subject to} \ \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} \newline \end{align*} \newline = \begin{cases} \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y}, & A^T \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{c} \leq \mathbf{0} \newline -\infty, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \end{align*}q(y)=​inf bTy+xT(ATy−c)subject to x≥0​={bTy,−∞,​ATy−c≤0otherwise​​

Therefore, the dual problem is,

max⁡ q(y)subject to ATy≤c\begin{align*} \max \ & q(\mathbf{y}) \newline \text{subject to} \ & A^T \mathbf{y} \leq \mathbf{c} \end{align*}max subject to ​q(y)ATy≤c​

which is exactly the dual LP problem defined above.

Remark 1

We note that the primal has nnn variables and mmm constraints, while the dual has mmm variables and nnn constraints.

Further, the dual of the dual is the primal, i.e., (D)(D)(D) is the dual of (P)(P)(P) and (P)(P)(P) is the dual of (D)(D)(D).

Proof

The Lagrangian for the dual LP problem is,

L(y,x)=bTy+xT(c−ATy)=cTx+yT(b−Ax)\begin{align*} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x}) & = \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y} + \mathbf{x}^T (\mathbf{c} - A^T \mathbf{y}) \newline & = \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}^T (\mathbf{b} - A \mathbf{x}) \end{align*}L(y,x)​=bTy+xT(c−ATy)=cTx+yT(b−Ax)​

thus, the dual function p(x)p(\mathbf{x})p(x) is,

p(x)=sup⁡ cTx+yT(b−Ax)subject to y free={cTx,Ax=b+∞,otherwise\begin{align*} \begin{align*} p(\mathbf{x}) = & \sup \ \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}^T (\mathbf{b} - A \mathbf{x}) \newline & \text{subject to} \ \mathbf{y} \text{ free} \newline \end{align*} \newline = \begin{cases} \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x}, & A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \newline +\infty, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \end{align*}p(x)=​sup cTx+yT(b−Ax)subject to y free​={cTx,+∞,​Ax=botherwise​​

Therefore, the dual problem is,

min⁡ p(x)subject to Ax=b x≥0\begin{align*} \min \ & p(\mathbf{x}) \newline \text{subject to} \ & A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \newline \ & \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} \end{align*}min subject to  ​p(x)Ax=bx≥0​

which is exactly the primal LP problem defined above, thus the dual of the dual is the primal.

Weak and Strong Duality

Theorem 1 (Weak Duality Theorem)

If x\mathbf{x}x is a feasible solution to the primal LP problem (P)(P)(P) and y\mathbf{y}y is a feasible solution to the dual LP problem (D)(D)(D), then,

cTx≥bTy\mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y}cTx≥bTy
Proof

Since x\mathbf{x}x is feasible for (P)(P)(P), we have that Ax=bA \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b and x≥0\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}x≥0.

Since y\mathbf{y}y is feasible for (D)(D)(D), we have that ATy≤cA^T \mathbf{y} \leq \mathbf{c}ATy≤c. Therefore,

cTx≥(ATy⏟≤c)Tx=yT(Ax)⏟=b=bTy■\begin{align*} \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} & \geq (\underbrace{A^T \mathbf{y}}_{\leq \mathbf{c}})^T \mathbf{x} \newline & = \mathbf{y}^T \underbrace{(A \mathbf{x})}_{= \mathbf{b}} \newline & = \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y} _\blacksquare \end{align*}cTx​≥(≤cATy​​)Tx=yT=b(Ax)​​=bTy■​​

From this theorem, we have some corollaries.

Corollary 1
  1. If z⋆=−∞z^{\star} = -\inftyz⋆=−∞ (the primal is unbounded), then the dual is infeasible.
  2. If q⋆=+∞q^{\star} = +\inftyq⋆=+∞ (the dual is unbounded), then the primal is infeasible.
Proof
  1. Suppose that the primal is unbounded, i.e., z⋆=−∞z^{\star} = -\inftyz⋆=−∞. If the dual is feasible, then there exists a feasible y\mathbf{y}y such that bTy\mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y}bTy is finite. However, by the weak duality theorem, we have that cTx≥bTy\mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y}cTx≥bTy for all feasible x\mathbf{x}x. This contradicts the assumption that the primal is unbounded, thus the dual must be infeasible.
  2. The proof is similar to the first part, by swapping the roles of the primal and dual.
  1. If x\mathbf{x}x is feasible to (P)(P)(P) and y\mathbf{y}y is feasible to (D)(D)(D) such that cTx=bTy\mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y}cTx=bTy, then x\mathbf{x}x and y\mathbf{y}y are optimal to (P)(P)(P) and (D)(D)(D), respectively.
Theorem 2 (Strong Duality Theorem)

If both (P)(P)(P) and (D)(D)(D) are both feasible, then there exists optimal x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ to (P)(P)(P) and optimal y⋆\mathbf{y}^{\star}y⋆ to (D)(D)(D) such that,

cTx⋆=bTy⋆\mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x}^{\star} = \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y}^{\star}cTx⋆=bTy⋆
Proof

Let (P)(P)(P) be feasible, thus z⋆>−∞z^{\star} > -\inftyz⋆>−∞, therefore, there exists an optimal BFS.

Let x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ be an optimal BFS to (P)(P)(P), for this BFS, we know that c~NT≥0\tilde{\mathbf{c}}_N^T \geq \mathbf{0}c~NT​≥0, let y⋆=cBTB−1\mathbf{y}^{\star} = \mathbf{c}_B^T B^{-1}y⋆=cBT​B−1.a

c~NT=cNT−cBTB−1N=cNT−y⋆TN≥0⇒NTy⋆≤cN\begin{align*} \tilde{\mathbf{c}}_N^T & = \mathbf{c}_N^T - \mathbf{c}_B^T B^{-1} N \newline & = \mathbf{c}_N^T - \mathbf{y}^{\star T} N \newline & \geq \mathbf{0} \newline \Rightarrow & N^T \mathbf{y}^{\star} \leq \mathbf{c}_N \newline \end{align*}c~NT​⇒​=cNT​−cBT​B−1N=cNT​−y⋆TN≥0NTy⋆≤cN​​

Furthermore,

cBT−y⋆TB=cBT−cBTB−1B⏟IBTy⋆=cB≤cB\begin{align*} \mathbf{c}_B^T - \mathbf{y}^{\star T} B & = \mathbf{c}_B^T - \mathbf{c}_B^T \underbrace{B^{-1} B}_{\mathbf{I}} \newline \mathbf{B}^T \mathbf{y}^{\star} & = \mathbf{c}_B \leq \mathbf{c}_B \newline \end{align*}cBT​−y⋆TBBTy⋆​=cBT​−cBT​IB−1B​​=cB​≤cB​​

This means that,

ATy⋆=[BTNT]y⋆=[BTyNTy]≤[cBcN]=c\begin{align*} A^T \mathbf{y}^{\star} & = \begin{bmatrix} B^T \newline N^T \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{y}^{\star} = \begin{bmatrix} B^T \mathbf{y} \newline N^T \mathbf{y} \end{bmatrix} \leq \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{c}_B \newline \mathbf{c}_N \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{c} \newline \end{align*}ATy⋆​=[BTNT​]y⋆=[BTyNTy​]≤[cB​cN​​]=c​

and thus y⋆\mathbf{y}^{\star}y⋆ is feasible for (D)(D)(D).

bTy⋆=y⋆Tb=cBTB−1b⏟xB=cBTxB⋆+cNTxN⋆=cTx⋆\begin{align*} \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{y}^{\star} & = \mathbf{y}^{\star T} \mathbf{b} \newline & = \underbrace{\mathbf{c}_B^T B^{-1} b}_{\mathbf{x}_B} \newline & = \mathbf{c}_B^T \mathbf{x}_B^{\star} + \mathbf{c}_N^T \mathbf{x}_N^{\star} \newline & = \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x}^\star \newline \end{align*}bTy⋆​=y⋆Tb=xB​cBT​B−1b​​=cBT​xB⋆​+cNT​xN⋆​=cTx⋆​

Thus, by corollary of weak duality, x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ and y⋆\mathbf{y}^{\star}y⋆ are optimal to (P)(P)(P) and (D)(D)(D), respectively. ■_\blacksquare■​

If x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ is an optimal BFS to (P)(P)(P) such that c~NT≥0\tilde{\mathbf{c}}_N^T \geq \mathbf{0}c~NT​≥0, then y⋆=(B−1)TcB\mathbf{y}^{\star} = (B^{-1})^T \mathbf{c}_By⋆=(B−1)TcB​ is optimal to (D)(D)(D).

Sensitivity Analysis

When solving LP problems, it is often the case that the parameters (i.e., AAA, b\mathbf{b}b, and c\mathbf{c}c) are not known exactly. Thus, it is important to understand how changes in these parameters affect the optimal solution and objective value. This is the goal of sensitivity analysis.

Definition 3 (Sensitivity Analysis in c\mathbf{c}c)

Let x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ (nondegenerate and unique) be an optimal BFS to the primal LP problem (P)(P)(P). Further, consider some arbitrary perturbation,

min⁡ (c+Δc)Txsubject to Ax=b x≥0\begin{align*} \min \ & (\mathbf{c} + \Delta \mathbf{c})^T \mathbf{x} \newline \text{subject to} \ & A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \newline \ & \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} \end{align*}min subject to  ​(c+Δc)TxAx=bx≥0​

x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ remains optimal if and only if,

c~NT=(cN+ΔcN)T−(cB+ΔcB)TB−1N≥0\tilde{\mathbf{c}}_N^T = (\mathbf{c}_N + \Delta \mathbf{c}_N)^T - (\mathbf{c}_B + \Delta \mathbf{c}_B)^T B^{-1} N \geq \mathbf{0}c~NT​=(cN​+ΔcN​)T−(cB​+ΔcB​)TB−1N≥0
Note

We would ideally like to define a function that maps,

w(c)=min⁡ cTxsubject to Ax=bx≥0w(\mathbf{c}) = \begin{align*} & \min \ \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \newline & \text{subject to} \ A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \newline & \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} \end{align*}w(c)=​min cTxsubject to Ax=bx≥0​

If Δc\Delta \mathbf{c}Δc is small enough, then we are interested in ∇cw(c)\nabla_{\mathbf{c}} w(\mathbf{c})∇c​w(c),

w(c)=cTx⋆∇cw(c)=x⋆\begin{align*} w(\mathbf{c}) & = \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x}^{\star} \newline \nabla_{\mathbf{c}} w(\mathbf{c}) & = \mathbf{x}^{\star} \end{align*}w(c)∇c​w(c)​=cTx⋆=x⋆​

However, this is only valid if x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ remains optimal for all small perturbations in c\mathbf{c}c.

Definition 4 (Sensitivity Analysis in b\mathbf{b}b)

Let x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ (nondegenerate and unique) be an optimal BFS to the primal LP problem (P)(P)(P). Further, consider some arbitrary perturbation,

min⁡ cTxsubject to Ax=b+Δb x≥0\begin{align*} \min \ & \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \newline \text{subject to} \ & A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} + \Delta \mathbf{b} \newline \ & \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} \end{align*}min subject to  ​cTxAx=b+Δbx≥0​

If for a given bbb the optimal BFS of (P)(P)(P) is nondegenerate and unique, then ∇bv(b)=y⋆\nabla_{\mathbf{b}} v(\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{y}^{\star}∇b​v(b)=y⋆, where y⋆\mathbf{y}^{\star}y⋆ is the optimal solution to the dual problem (D)(D)(D).

Complementarity

Theorem 4 (Complementary Slackness Theorem)

Let x\mathbf{x}x be feasible to the primal LP problem (P)(P)(P) and y\mathbf{y}y be feasible to the dual LP problem (D)(D)(D).
