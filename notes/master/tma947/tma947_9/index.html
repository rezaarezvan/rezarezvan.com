Introduction

In this part we will cover the concept of Linear Programming (LP) and its standard form (we briefly introduced this last part, but we will formalize it here). We will then introduce the concept of Basic (Feasible) Solutions, which is a very important concept in LP theory. Finally, we will talk about the idea(s) that will lead us to the Simplex method, which is a very efficient method for solving LPs in practice.

Linear Programming

Definition 1 (Linear Programming)

A linear program (LP) is a problem of the form,

min⁡ cTxsubject to x∈P\begin{align*} \min \ & \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \newline \text{subject to} \ & \mathbf{x} \in P \end{align*}min subject to ​cTxx∈P​

where P⊆RnP \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nP⊆Rn is a polyhedron.

Recall (Polyhedron)

A polyhedron P⊆RnP \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nP⊆Rn is a set of the form,

P={x∈Rn∣A~x≤b~},P = \{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid \tilde{A} \mathbf{x} \leq \tilde{b}\},P={x∈Rn∣A~x≤b~},

for some A~∈Rm×n\tilde{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}A~∈Rm×n and b~∈Rm\tilde{b} \in \mathbb{R}^mb~∈Rm.

Remark 1 (Observations on properties of LPs)
  1. We see that the polyhedron PPP is the set of the intersection of half-spaces, which means that it is a convex set.
  2. The boundaries are straight lines (hyperplanes in higher dimensions).
  3. There will always be a finite number of extreme points (corners) in a polyhedron.
  4. The polyhedron can be unbounded (extend to infinity in some direction).
Intuition (Geometric Intuition of LPs)

Imagine if we take a (hyper)plane perpendicular to the negative gradient of the objective function, i.e., perpendicular to −∇x(cTx)=−c-\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} (\mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x}) = -\mathbf{c}−∇x​(cTx)=−c, as far as possible while still touching the polyhedron PPP.

The optimal solution to the LP should exist in an extreme point (corner) of the polyhedron PPP where the (hyper)plane touches it (we will prove this later).

Standard Form

Definition 2 (Standard Form of a Linear Program)

An LP on standard form is,

min⁡ cTxsubject to Ax=b x≥0\begin{align*} \min \ & \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \newline \text{subject to} \ & A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \newline \ & \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} \end{align*}min subject to  ​cTxAx=bx≥0​
Note

Any Linear Program can be converted to standard form, we will not prove this, but let’s take an example to illustrate how we can do this.

Example 1 (Converting to Standard Form)

Convert the following LP to standard form,

min⁡ x2subject to x1−x2≤1 x1+x2≥1 x1,x2≥0\begin{align*} \min \ & x_2 \newline \text{subject to} \ & x_1 - x_2 \leq 1 \newline \ & x_1 + x_2 \geq 1 \newline \ & x_1, x_2 \geq 0 \end{align*}min subject to   ​x2​x1​−x2​≤1x1​+x2​≥1x1​,x2​≥0​
Solution

We start by rewriting the inequalities as equalities by introducing slack variables.

Slack variables are variables that “take up the slack” in an inequality constraint to turn it into an equality constraint (i.e., positive slack variables for ≤\leq≤ constraints and negative slack variables for ≥\geq≥ constraints).

x1−x2+s1=1x1+x2−s2=1x1,x2,s1,s2≥0\begin{align*} x_1 - x_2 + s_1 & = 1 \newline x_1 + x_2 - s_2 & = 1 \newline x_1, x_2, s_1, s_2 & \geq 0 \end{align*}x1​−x2​+s1​x1​+x2​−s2​x1​,x2​,s1​,s2​​=1=1≥0​

where s1,s2≥0s_1, s_2 \geq 0s1​,s2​≥0 are slack

Now, we can rewrite everything in matrix form,

x=[x1x2s1s2],c=[0100],A=[1−110110−1],b=[11]\begin{align*} \mathbf{x} & = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \newline x_2 \newline s_1 \newline s_2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{c} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \newline 1 \newline 0 \newline 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 & 1 & 0 \newline 1 & 1 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \newline 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}x​=​x1​x2​s1​s2​​​,c=​0100​​,A=[11​−11​10​0−1​],b=[11​]​

Thus, the LP in standard form is,

min⁡ cTxsubject to Ax=b x≥0\begin{align*} \min \ & \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \newline \text{subject to} \ & A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \newline \ & \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} \end{align*}min subject to  ​cTxAx=bx≥0​

Basic (Feasible) Solutions

Definition 3 (Basic Solution)

A point xˉ\bar{\mathbf{x}}xˉ is called a basic solution if,

  1. Axˉ=bA \bar{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{b}Axˉ=b,
  2. The columns of AAA corresponding to the non-zero components of xˉ\bar{\mathbf{x}}xˉ are linearly independent.
Intuition (Procedure to find Basic Solutions)
  1. Choose mmm linearly independent columns of A∈Rm×nA \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}A∈Rm×n where m≤nm \leq nm≤n.
  2. Rearrange the columns so that,
A=[BN],A = \begin{bmatrix} B & N \end{bmatrix},A=[B​N​],

where B∈Rm×mB \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}B∈Rm×m matrix with the chosen columns (basic variables) and N∈Rm×(n−m)N \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times (n - m)}N∈Rm×(n−m) matrix with the remaining columns (non-basic variables).

Thus, linear independence   ⟺  rank(B)=m  ⟺  B\iff \mathrm{rank}(B) = m \iff B⟺rank(B)=m⟺B is invertible. 3. Rearrange x\mathbf{x}x so that,

x=[xBxN],\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_B \newline \mathbf{x}_N \end{bmatrix},x=[xB​xN​​],

this means that,

Ax=BxB+NxNA \mathbf{x} = B \mathbf{x}_B + N \mathbf{x}_NAx=BxB​+NxN​

  1. Set xN=0\mathbf{x}_N = \mathbf{0}xN​=0.

  2. Set xB=B−1b\mathbf{x}_B = B^{-1} \mathbf{b}xB​=B−1b.

Note

One property of this procedure is that, there at most (nm)=n!m!(n−m)!\binom{n}{m} = \frac{n!}{m!(n - m)!}(mn​)=m!(n−m)!n!​ basic solutions (since this is the number of ways to choose mmm columns from nnn columns).

Definition 4 (Basic Feasible Solution)

A point xˉ\bar{\mathbf{x}}xˉ is called a basic feasible solution (BFS), if,

  1. xˉ\bar{\mathbf{x}}xˉ is a basic solution,
  2. xˉ≥0\bar{\mathbf{x}} \geq \mathbf{0}xˉ≥0.
Note

A BFS is a point in the feasible region (polyhedron) P={x∈Rn∣Ax=b, x≥0}P = \{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}, \ \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\}P={x∈Rn∣Ax=b, x≥0}.

Theorem 1 (Extreme Points and Basic Feasible Solutions)

Let AAA have rank(A)=m\mathrm{rank}(A) = mrank(A)=m. x\mathbf{x}x is an extreme point of P={x∈Rn∣Ax=b, x≥0}P = \{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}, \ \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\}P={x∈Rn∣Ax=b, x≥0} if and only if x\mathbf{x}x is a basic feasible solution.

Theorem 2 (Optimal Solutions and Basic Feasible Solutions)

Let,

z⋆=inf⁡ cTxsubject to x∈Pz^{\star} = \begin{align*} \inf \ & \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x} \newline \text{subject to} \ & \mathbf{x} \in P \end{align*}z⋆=inf subject to ​cTxx∈P​

where P={x∈Rn∣Ax=b, x≥0}P = \{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}, \ \mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\}P={x∈Rn∣Ax=b, x≥0}.

  1. z⋆z^{\star}z⋆ is finite if and only if PPP is non-empty and cTd≥0\mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{d} \geq 0cTd≥0 for all d∈{v∈Rn∣Av=0, v≥0}\mathbf{d} \in \{\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid A \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}, \ \mathbf{v} \geq \mathbf{0}\}d∈{v∈Rn∣Av=0, v≥0}.

  2. If z⋆z^{\star}z⋆ is finite, then there exists an optimal solution among the extreme points of PPP (i.e., among the BFSs).

Note

Other points then extreme points might also be optimal solutions, but there will always be an optimal solution that is an extreme point (BFS).

Ideas Leading to the Simplex Method

We will now discuss some ideas that will lead us to the Simplex method, which is a very efficient method for solving LPs in practice.

Definition 5 (Degenerate)

A BFS xˉ\bar{\mathbf{x}}xˉ is called degenerate if, two partitions [BN]\begin{bmatrix} B & N \end{bmatrix}[B​N​] and [B~N~]\begin{bmatrix} \tilde{B} & \tilde{N} \end{bmatrix}[B~​N~​] corresponds to xˉ\bar{\mathbf{x}}xˉ.

Equivalently, a BFS is degenerate if some basic variable is zero.

Definition 6 (Adjacent)

Two BFSs x1\mathbf{x}_1x1​ and x2\mathbf{x}_2x2​ are called adjacent if,

∀y∈αx1+(1−α)x2, α∈(0,1),y=λu+(1−λ)v, u,v∈P, λ∈(0,1)  ⟹  {u=λux1+(1−λu)x2,v=λvx1+(1−λv)x2,\begin{align*} \forall \mathbf{y} & \in \alpha \mathbf{x}_1 + (1 - \alpha) \mathbf{x}_2, \ \alpha \in (0, 1), \newline \mathbf{y} & = \lambda \mathbf{u} + (1 - \lambda) \mathbf{v}, \ \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in P, \ \lambda \in (0, 1) \newline & \implies \newline & \begin{cases} \mathbf{u} = \lambda_{\mathbf{u}} \mathbf{x}_1 + (1 - \lambda_{\mathbf{u}}) \mathbf{x}_2, \newline \mathbf{v} = \lambda_{\mathbf{v}} \mathbf{x}_1 + (1 - \lambda_{\mathbf{v}}) \mathbf{x}_2, \newline \end{cases} \end{align*}∀yy​∈αx1​+(1−α)x2​, α∈(0,1),=λu+(1−λ)v, u,v∈P, λ∈(0,1)⟹{u=λu​x1​+(1−λu​)x2​,v=λv​x1​+(1−λv​)x2​,​​

or simply, for two vertices to be adjacent, the line segment connecting them must lie on the boundary of the polyhedron (i.e., it cannot pass through the interior of the polyhedron).

For example, the points (1,0)(1, 0)(1,0) and (0,0)(0, 0)(0,0) are adjacent, but the points (1,0)(1, 0)(1,0) and (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1) are not.

Theorem 3 (Characterization of Adjacent BFSs)

Let x1\mathbf{x}_1x1​ and x2\mathbf{x}_2x2​ be two different BFS. Let them correspond to [B1N1]\begin{bmatrix} B_1 & N_1 \end{bmatrix}[B1​​N1​​] and [B2N2]\begin{bmatrix} B_2 & N_2 \end{bmatrix}[B2​​N2​​], respectively. Assume that all columns but one in B1B_1B1​ and B2B_2B2​ are the same.

Then, x1\mathbf{x}_1x1​ and x2\mathbf{x}_2x2​ are adjacent BFSs.

Intuition (Algorithmic Idea to Move Between Adjacent BFSs)

Lastly, we will discuss the blueprint for an algorithm to find an optimal solution to an LP.

  1. Start at a BFS.
  2. Move to adjacent BFS for which the objective function is improved (decreased for minimization problems).
  3. Repeat step 2 until no adjacent BFS improves the objective function.
