Introduction

In this part, we will define some basics of probability theory that are essential for understanding advanced probabilistic machine learning.

Firstly, some notation.

Notation
  • Vectors: Bold lowercase, e.g., x\mathbf{x}x
  • Matrices: Bold uppercase, e.g., X\mathbf{X}X
  • Random variables, vectors, and matrices: Sansserif font, e.g., X\mathsf{X}X, x\mathsf{\mathbf{x}}x, X\mathsf{\mathbf{X}}X
  • Sets: Calligraphic font, e.g., X\mathcal{X}X
Definition 1 (Discrete Random Variable)

Probability mass function, px(x)=p(x)p_{\mathsf{x}}(x) = p(x)px​(x)=p(x), with,

0≤p(x)≤1,∑x∈Xp(x)=10 \leq p(x) \leq 1, \quad \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) = 10≤p(x)≤1,x∈X∑​p(x)=1

The joint distribution of two discrete random variables x\mathsf{x}x and y\mathsf{y}y is defined as,

px,y(x,y)=p(x,y)p_{\mathsf{x},\mathsf{y}}(x, y) = p(x, y)px,y​(x,y)=p(x,y)

The conditional distribution of x\mathsf{x}x given y\mathsf{y}y is defined as,

px∣y(x∣y)=p(x∣y)p_{\mathsf{x} \mid \mathsf{y}}(x \mid y) = p(x \mid y)px∣y​(x∣y)=p(x∣y)

The marginal probability distribution of x\mathsf{x}x can be found from the joint distribution as,

p(x)=∑y∈Yp(x,y)p(x) = \sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} p(x, y)p(x)=y∈Y∑​p(x,y)

In general,

p(x1,…,xi−1,xi+1,…,xn)=∑xi∈Xip(x1,…,xn)p(x_1, \ldots, x_{i - 1}, x_{i + 1}, \ldots, x_n) = \sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{X}_i} p(x_1, \ldots, x_n)p(x1​,…,xi−1​,xi+1​,…,xn​)=xi​∈Xi​∑​p(x1​,…,xn​)

Lastly, Bayes’ theorem states that,

p(x∣y)≔p(y∣x)p(x)p(y)p(x \mid y) \coloneqq \frac{p(y \mid x) p(x)}{p(y)}p(x∣y):=p(y)p(y∣x)p(x)​

Recall (Expectation, Variance, and Covariance)

The expectation (or, the average value of f(x)f(x)f(x) under probability distribution p(x)p(x)p(x)) is defined as,

The sample mean, given NNN points {xi}\{x_i\}{xi​} drawn from p(x)p(x)p(x), is defined as,

E[f(x)]≃1N∑i=1Nf(xi)\mathbb{E}[f(\mathsf{x})] \simeq \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i)E[f(x)]≃N1​i=1∑N​f(xi​)

with,

lim⁡N→∞1N∑i=1Nf(xi)=E[f(x)]\lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i) = \mathbb{E}[f(\mathsf{x})]N→∞lim​N1​i=1∑N​f(xi​)=E[f(x)]

For expectations of functions of several variables, we will keep the subscript to indicate the variable averaged over, e.g.,

Ex[f(x,y)]orEx∼p(x)[f(x,y)]\mathbb{E}_{\mathsf{x}}[f(\mathsf{x}, \mathsf{y})] \quad \text{or} \quad \mathbb{E}_{\mathsf{x} \sim p(x)}[f(\mathsf{x}, \mathsf{y})]Ex​[f(x,y)]orEx∼p(x)​[f(x,y)]

Further, the conditional expectation is defined as,

Ex∼p(x∣y)[f(x∣y)]≔∑xp(x∣y)f(x∣y)\mathbb{E}_{\mathsf{x} \sim p(x \mid y)}[f(\mathsf{x} \mid y)] \coloneqq \sum_x p(x \mid y) f(x \mid y)Ex∼p(x∣y)​[f(x∣y)]:=x∑​p(x∣y)f(x∣y)

The variance (how much variability there is in f(x)f(x)f(x) around its expected value) is defined as,

Var[f(x)]≔E[(f(x)−E[f(x)])2]\mathrm{Var}[f(\mathsf{x})] \coloneqq \mathbb{E}[(f(\mathsf{x}) - \mathbb{E}[f(\mathsf{x})])^2]Var[f(x)]:=E[(f(x)−E[f(x)])2]

Thus, the variance of x\mathsf{x}x is,

Var[x]=E[x2]−(E[x])2\mathrm{Var}[\mathsf{x}] = \mathbb{E}[\mathsf{x}^2] - (\mathbb{E}[\mathsf{x}])^2Var[x]=E[x2]−(E[x])2

Finally, the covariance of x\mathsf{x}x and y\mathsf{y}y (the extent to which x\mathsf{x}x and y\mathsf{y}y vary together) is defined as,

Cov[x,y]≔Ex,y[(x−E[x])(y−E[y])]=Ex,y[xy]−E[x]E[y]\begin{align*} \mathrm{Cov}[\mathsf{x}, \mathsf{y}] & \coloneqq \mathbb{E}_{\mathsf{x}, \mathsf{y}}[(\mathsf{x} - \mathbb{E}[\mathsf{x}])(\mathsf{y} - \mathbb{E}[\mathsf{y}])] \newline & = \mathbb{E}_{\mathsf{x}, \mathsf{y}}[\mathsf{x} \mathsf{y}] - \mathbb{E}[\mathsf{x}] \mathbb{E}[\mathsf{y}] \end{align*}Cov[x,y]​:=Ex,y​[(x−E[x])(y−E[y])]=Ex,y​[xy]−E[x]E[y]​

The covariance of two random vectors is defined as,

Cov[x,y]≔Ex,y[(x−E[x])(yT−E[yT])]\mathrm{Cov}[\mathsf{\mathbf{x}}, \mathsf{\mathbf{y}}] \coloneqq \mathbb{E}_{\mathsf{\mathbf{x}}, \mathsf{\mathbf{y}}}[(\mathsf{\mathbf{x}} - \mathbb{E}[\mathsf{\mathbf{x}}])(\mathsf{\mathbf{y}^T} - \mathbb{E}[\mathsf{\mathbf{y}^T}])]Cov[x,y]:=Ex,y​[(x−E[x])(yT−E[yT])]

Probabilities: Frequentist VS. Bayesian

In the frequentist interpretation, the relative frequency of occurrence of an outcome after repeating an experiment a large number of times defines the probability of that outcome,

p≔lim⁡N→∞kNp \coloneqq \lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{k}{N}p:=N→∞lim​Nk​

Only repeatable random events have probabilities in this interpretation!

However, in the Bayesian interpretation, it quantifies the uncertainty of events happening. The probability is a measure of the belief or confidence regarding the occurrence of an event. The prior belief about an event will likely change when new information is revealed.

Example 1

Assume that 90% of people with Kreuzfeld-Jacob (KJ) disease [^1] ate hamburgers. The probability of an individual to have KJ is one in 100,000.

Assuming half of the population eat hamburgers, what is the probability that a hamburger eater will have KJ disease?

Firstly, we define the events having KJ disease as KJKJKJ and eating hamburgers as HHH.

Thus, we want to know p(KJ=yes∣H=yes)p(KJ = \text{yes} \mid H = \text{yes})p(KJ=yes∣H=yes).

Consider the following population of one million people.

From the table we see that,
p(KJ=yes)=106×1105=10p(H=yes∣KJ=yes)=910=0.9p(H=yes)=50%\imples500,000 people\begin{align*} p(KJ = \text{yes}) & = 10^6 \times \frac{1}{10^5} = 10 \newline p(H = \text{yes} \mid KJ = \text{yes}) & = \frac{9}{10} = 0.9 \newline p(H = \text{yes}) & = 50\% \imples 500,000 \text{ people} \end{align*}p(KJ=yes)p(H=yes∣KJ=yes)p(H=yes)​=106×1051​=10=109​=0.9=50%\imples500,000 people​

Now,

Intuition (Bayesian Approach)

In the Bayesian approach, we start with our hypothesis x\mathsf{x}x (e.g., patient has a disease or not) and our data y\mathsf{y}y (e.g., test result or patient symptoms).

p(x∣y)=p(y∣x)p(x)p(y)p(\mathsf{x} \mid \mathsf{y}) = \frac{p(\mathsf{y} \mid \mathsf{x}) p(\mathsf{x})}{p(\mathsf{y})}p(x∣y)=p(y)p(y∣x)p(x)​

We call p(x)p(\mathsf{x})p(x) our prior belief in the hypothesis before looking at any data. p(y∣x)p(\mathsf{y} \mid \mathsf{x})p(y∣x) is the likelihood of the data if the hypothesis is true. p(y)p(\mathsf{y})p(y) is called the marginal likelihood (commoness of the data). Finally, p(x∣y)p(\mathsf{x} \mid \mathsf{y})p(x∣y) is our posterior belief in the hypothesis after seeing the data.

Consider the following setup. Let D≔{x1,…,xN}\mathcal{D} \coloneqq \{\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_N\}D:={x1​,…,xN​} be our observed variables (the data) and θ≔(θ1,…,θM)\boldsymbol{\theta} \coloneqq (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_M)θ:=(θ1​,…,θM​) be our latent variables (the parameters we want to learn/find). In probabilistic modeling, we treat both observed and latent variables as random variables.

Can we model a relationship between D\mathcal{D}D and θ\boldsymbol{\theta}θ via p(D∣θ)p(\mathcal{D} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})p(D∣θ)?

Many inference problems are of the form,

p(θ∣D)=p(D∣θ)p(θ)p(D)p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta})}{p(\mathcal{D})}p(θ∣D)=p(D)p(D∣θ)p(θ)​

Seeing quantities as functions of θ\boldsymbol{\theta}θ, p(D)p(\mathcal{D})p(D) can be viewed as a normaliziation constant and we can rewrite the equation as,

p(θ∣D)∝p(D∣θ)p(θ)p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) p(\boldsymbol{\theta})p(θ∣D)∝p(D∣θ)p(θ)

Most probable a posterior (maximum a posterior, MAP) setting,

θMAP⋆=arg⁡max⁡θ p(θ∣D).\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\star}_{\text{MAP}} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg \max} \ p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathcal{D}).θMAP⋆​=θargmax​ p(θ∣D).

If p(θ)p(\boldsymbol{\theta})p(θ) is constant (i.e., uniform prior), then the MAP estimate reduces to the maximum likelihood (ML) estimate,

θML⋆=arg⁡max⁡θ p(D∣θ).\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\star}_{\text{ML}} = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg \max} \ p(\mathcal{D} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}).θML⋆​=θargmax​ p(D∣θ).
Example 2 (Tossing a Biased Coin)

Imagine we have a random variable x∈{0,1}\mathsf{x} \in \{0, 1\}x∈{0,1} representing the outcome of tossing a (biased) coin flip (0 = tails, 1 = heads) with unknown bias μ∈[0,1]\mu \in [0, 1]μ∈[0,1] (i.e., the probability of getting heads).

p(x=1)=μ,p(x=0)=1−μp(\mathsf{x} = 1) = \mu, \quad p(\mathsf{x} = 0) = 1 - \mup(x=1)=μ,p(x=0)=1−μ

Thus, the goal is, given a data set D={x1,…,xN}\mathcal{D} = \{x_1, \ldots, x_N\}D={x1​,…,xN​} estimate μ\muμ, i.e., the probability that a toss will result in heads, p(μ∣D)p(\mu \mid \mathcal{D})p(μ∣D).

By applying Bayes’ theorem, we have,

p(μ∣D)=p(D∣μ)p(μ)p(D)∝p(D∣μ)p(μ)p(\mu \mid \mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mu) p(\mu)}{p(\mathcal{D})} \propto p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mu) p(\mu)p(μ∣D)=p(D)p(D∣μ)p(μ)​∝p(D∣μ)p(μ)

We note that a single coin toss corresponds to a Bernoulli random variable,

p(x∣μ)=Bern(x;μ)=μx(1−μ)1−xp(\mathsf{x} \mid \mu) = \mathrm{Bern}(\mathsf{x}; \mu) = \mu^{\mathsf{x}} (1 - \mu)^{1 - \mathsf{x}}p(x∣μ)=Bern(x;μ)=μx(1−μ)1−x

with,

E[x]=μVar[x]=μ(1−μ)\begin{align*} \mathbb{E}[\mathsf{x}] & = \mu \newline \mathrm{Var}[\mathsf{x}] & = \mu (1 - \mu) \end{align*}E[x]Var[x]​=μ=μ(1−μ)​

Thus, for NNN independent coin tosses,

p(D∣μ)=∏i=1Np(xi∣μ)=∏i=1Nμxi(1−μ)1−xi=μ∑i=1Nxi(1−μ)N−∑i=1Nxi=μh(1−μ)N−h\begin{align*} p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mu) & = \prod_{i=1}^{N} p(x_i \mid \mu) \newline & = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \mu^{x_i} (1 - \mu)^{1 - x_i} \newline & = \mu^{\sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i} (1 - \mu)^{N - \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i} \newline & = \mu^{h} (1 - \mu)^{N - h} \end{align*}p(D∣μ)​=i=1∏N​p(xi​∣μ)=i=1∏N​μxi​(1−μ)1−xi​=μ∑i=1N​xi​(1−μ)N−∑i=1N​xi​=μh(1−μ)N−h​

where hhh is the number of heads observed in the data set D\mathcal{D}D. To be explicit, how we went from a product to a sum is by taking the logarithm of the product,

log⁡p(D∣μ)=∑i=1Nlog⁡p(xi∣μ)=∑i=1Nlog⁡(μxi(1−μ)1−xi)=∑i=1N(xilog⁡μ+(1−xi)log⁡(1−μ))=∑i=1Nxilog⁡μ+∑i=1N(1−xi)log⁡(1−μ)\begin{align*} \log p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mu) & = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log p(x_i \mid \mu) \newline & = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log \left( \mu^{x_i} (1 - \mu)^{1 - x_i} \right) \newline & = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( x_i \log \mu + (1 - x_i) \log(1 - \mu) \right) \newline & = \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i \log \mu + \sum_{i=1}^{N} (1 - x_i) \log(1 - \mu) \newline \end{align*}logp(D∣μ)​=i=1∑N​logp(xi​∣μ)=i=1∑N​log(μxi​(1−μ)1−xi​)=i=1∑N​(xi​logμ+(1−xi​)log(1−μ))=i=1∑N​xi​logμ+i=1∑N​(1−xi​)log(1−μ)​

In the Bayesian approach,

p(μ∣D)≔p(D∣μ)p(μ)p(D)∝p(D∣μ)p(μ)p(\mu \mid \mathcal{D}) \coloneqq \frac{p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mu) p(\mu)}{p(\mathcal{D})} \propto p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mu) p(\mu)p(μ∣D):=p(D)p(D∣μ)p(μ)​∝p(D∣μ)p(μ)

Now, if we now consider a continuum of parameters?

A flat (uniform) prior p(μ)=kp(\mu) = kp(μ)=k,

∫p(μ) dμ=1  ⟹  ∫01p(μ) dμ=k=1\int p(\mu) \ d\mu = 1 \implies \int_0^1 p(\mu) \ d\mu = k = 1∫p(μ) dμ=1⟹∫01​p(μ) dμ=k=1

p(μ∣D)∝p(D∣μ)p(μ)=μh(1−μ)N−h⋅1=μh(1−μ)N−hp(\mu \mid \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mu) p(\mu) = \mu^{h} (1 - \mu)^{N - h} \cdot 1 = \mu^{h} (1 - \mu)^{N - h}p(μ∣D)∝p(D∣μ)p(μ)=μh(1−μ)N−h⋅1=μh(1−μ)N−h

p(μ∣D)=1cp(D∣μ)p(μ)=1cμh(1−μ)N−hp(\mu \mid \mathcal{D}) = \frac{1}{c} p(\mathcal{D} \mid \mu) p(\mu) = \frac{1}{c} \mu^{h} (1 - \mu)^{N - h}p(μ∣D)=c1​p(D∣μ)p(μ)=c1​μh(1−μ)N−h

where the constant ccc is obtained as,

c=∫01μh(1−μ)N−h dμ≡B(h+1,N−h+1)c = \int_0^1 \mu^{h} (1 - \mu)^{N - h} \ d\mu \equiv \mathrm{B}(h + 1, N - h + 1)c=∫01​μh(1−μ)N−h dμ≡B(h+1,N−h+1)

B(α,β)=∫01μα−1(1−μ)β−1 dt\mathrm{B}(\alpha, \beta) = \int_0^1 \mu^{\alpha - 1} (1 - \mu)^{\beta - 1} \ dtB(α,β)=∫01​μα−1(1−μ)β−1 dt

Beta(μ;α,β)=1B(α,β)μα−1(1−μ)β−1=Γ(α+β)Γ(α)Γ(β)μα−1(1−μ)β−1\begin{align*} \mathrm{Beta}(\mu; \alpha, \beta) & = \frac{1}{\mathrm{B}(\alpha, \beta)} \mu^{\alpha - 1} (1 - \mu)^{\beta - 1} \newline & = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha + \beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(\beta)} \mu^{\alpha - 1} (1 - \mu)^{\beta - 1} \end{align*}Beta(μ;α,β)​=B(α,β)1​μα−1(1−μ)β−1=Γ(α)Γ(β)Γ(α+β)​μα−1(1−μ)β−1​

Further, we can see that, if a prior is proportional to powers of μ\muμ and (1−μ)(1 - \mu)(1−μ), then the posterior distribution will have the same functional form as the prior!

A conjugate prior (posterior will be of same functional form as prior),

p(μ)=Beta(μ;α,β)=Γ(α+β)Γ(α)Γ(β)μα−1(1−μ)β−1p(\mu) = \mathrm{Beta}(\mu; \alpha, \beta) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha + \beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(\beta)} \mu^{\alpha - 1} (1 - \mu)^{\beta - 1}p(μ)=Beta(μ;α,β)=Γ(α)Γ(β)Γ(α+β)​μα−1(1−μ)β−1

Then,

Thus, the posterior is also a beta distribution!

where a′=h+aa^{\prime} = h + aa′=h+a and b′=N−h+bb^{\prime} = N - h + bb′=N−h+b. If we do now know anything about the coin, we can choose a uniform prior, However, our posterior can act as a prior if we subseqently observe additional data, i.e., sequential inference.

Summary

Summary

We conclude this by (re)stating the definition of probabilistic machine learning.

  • We treat models and its parameters as random variables.
  • Learning does not provide a single model, but a distribution of likely models.
  • Can incorporate prior knowledge on model and parameters.
