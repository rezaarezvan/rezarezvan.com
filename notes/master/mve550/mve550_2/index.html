Introduction to Bayesian Inference

In this part, we will introduce and discuss the idea of Bayesian inference, which is predicting from conditional stochastic models. Further, we will introduce and discuss a practical example of conjugacy in Bayesian inference. Lastly, we will briefly discuss the computations of predictive distributions and Bayesian inference in a discrete setting, or with numerical integration.

Bayesian Inference

Example: Throwing a Die

If you are throwing a fair six-sided dice, your stochastic model would be that each outcome has a probability of 16\frac{1}{6}61​. New observations would be independent of old observations, i.e., to make predictions, you do not need (new) data.

Assume instead the dice may be biased in some unknown way. A way to make predictions would be to first acquire data, i.e., record how often each outcome occurs, and use that information when predicting. Thus, outcomes would be dependent. Further, you would use a more complex stochastic model that reasonably models the dependency.

Given a sequence D≔{1,5,6,1,3,1,1,2,1,5}\mathcal{D} \coloneqq \{1, 5, 6, 1, 3, 1, 1, 2, 1, 5\}D:={1,5,6,1,3,1,1,2,1,5}, the probability for 111 in the next throw is then computed as,

P(1∣D)=P(D∣1)P(1)P(D)P(1 \mid \mathcal{D}) = \frac{P(\mathcal{D} \mid 1) P(1)}{P(\mathcal{D})}P(1∣D)=P(D)P(D∣1)P(1)​

Now imagine a scenario when dealing with a biased coin. The prior used is that θ\thetaθ, the probability of heads, is either 0.70.70.7 or 0.50.50.5, with equal probability.

[!INTUITION/Reformulation using the underlying parameter   θ\thetaθ] A more common approach is to define the model in terms of a parameter θ\thetaθ, so that all observations are independent given the parameter.

In our dice example, θ\thetaθ is a discrete random variable, the possible values are 0.70.70.7 and 0.30.30.3,

π(θ=0.7)=π(θ=0.5)=0.5\pi(\theta = 0.7) = \pi(\theta = 0.5) = 0.5π(θ=0.7)=π(θ=0.5)=0.5

Notation: General Terminology in Bayesian Inference

The probability distribution for the parameter θ\thetaθ,

π(θ)\pi(\theta)π(θ)

is called the prior distribution.

The probability distribution for the data yyy given the parameter θ\thetaθ,

π(y∣θ)\pi(y \mid \theta)π(y∣θ)

is called the likelihood.

Lastly, the probability distribution for the parameter θ\thetaθ given the data yyy,

π(θ∣y)\pi(\theta \mid y)π(θ∣y)

is called the posterior distribution.

Definition: Conjugate Priors

Given a likelihood model π(x∣θ)\pi(x \mid \theta)π(x∣θ). A conjugate family of priors to this likelihood is a parametric family of distributions for θ\thetaθ so that if the prior is in this family, the posterior of the form θ∣x\theta \mid xθ∣x is also in the family.

π(x∣θ)=e−θθxx!.\pi(x \mid \theta) = e^{-\theta} \frac{\theta^x}{x!}.π(x∣θ)=e−θx!θx​.

Γ(θ;α,B)=BαΓ(α)θα−1exp⁡(−Bθ).\mathrm{\Gamma}(\theta; \alpha, \Beta) = \frac{\Beta^{\alpha}}{\Gamma(\alpha)} \theta^{\alpha - 1} \exp(-\Beta \theta).Γ(θ;α,B)=Γ(α)Bα​θα−1exp(−Bθ).

Bayesian Inference in Discrete Settings

Finally, a word on Bayesian inference in discrete settings.

If the sample space θ\thetaθ is finite, Bayesian inference is quite easy.

  • The prior distribution π(θ)\pi(\theta)π(θ) is represented by a vector.
  • The posterior distribution π(θ∣y)\pi(\theta \mid y)π(θ∣y) is obtained by termwise multiplication of the vectors π(y∣θ)\pi(y \mid \theta)π(y∣θ) and π(θ)\pi(\theta)π(θ), followed by normalization.
  • The prediction π(ynew∣y)=∫θπ(ynew∣θ)π(θ∣y) dθ\pi(y_{\text{new}} \mid y) = \int_{\theta} \pi(y_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \pi(\theta \mid y) \ d\thetaπ(ynew​∣y)=∫θ​π(ynew​∣θ)π(θ∣y) dθ simplifies to taking the sum of the termwise product of the vectors π(ynew∣θ)\pi(y_{\text{new}} \mid \theta)π(ynew​∣θ) and π(θ∣y)\pi(\theta \mid y)π(θ∣y).

Finally, the prediction we want to make can be expressed as a quotient of integrals,

π(ynew∣y)=∫θπ(ynew∣θ)π(θ∣y) dθ=∫θπ(ynew∣θ)π(y∣θ)π(θ)∫θπ(y∣θ)π(θ) dθ dθ=∫θπ(ynew∣θ)π(y∣θ)π(θ) dθ∫θπ(y∣θ)π(θ) dθ\begin{align*} \pi(y_{\text{new}} \mid y) & = \int_{\theta} \pi(y_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \pi(\theta \mid y) \ d\theta \newline & = \int_{\theta} \pi(y_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \frac{\pi(y \mid \theta) \pi(\theta)}{\int_{\theta} \pi(y \mid \theta) \pi(\theta) \ d\theta} \ d\theta \newline & = \frac{\int_{\theta} \pi(y_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \pi(y \mid \theta) \pi(\theta) \ d\theta}{\int_{\theta} \pi(y \mid \theta) \pi(\theta) \ d\theta} \newline \end{align*}π(ynew​∣y)​=∫θ​π(ynew​∣θ)π(θ∣y) dθ=∫θ​π(ynew​∣θ)∫θ​π(y∣θ)π(θ) dθπ(y∣θ)π(θ)​ dθ=∫θ​π(y∣θ)π(θ) dθ∫θ​π(ynew​∣θ)π(y∣θ)π(θ) dθ​​
