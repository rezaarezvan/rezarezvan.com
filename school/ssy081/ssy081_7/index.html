Part 7 - Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem

Date: 9/21/2023

Introduction

In this part we’ll cover the important Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem. This is a fundamental theorem within digital signal processing.

However, to understand the theorem, we firstly need to understand filters.

Transfer function

Say we have a system which can be modeled as:

y(t)=f(t)∗h(t)y(t) = f(t) * h(t)y(t)=f(t)∗h(t)

The same system, but in the frequency domain is therefore:

Y(ω)=F(ω) H(ω)Y(\omega) = F(\omega)\ H(\omega)Y(ω)=F(ω) H(ω)

Just as we call, h(t)h(t)h(t), the system response function, we call H(ω)H(\omega)H(ω) for the spectral response of the system.

Since, in the most general case, we view our frequency functions as complex functions. We can say that

An input signals spectral component of frequency, ω\omegaω, is modified in amplitude by a factor of, ∣H(ω)∣|H(\omega)|∣H(ω)∣ and shifted in phase by an angle of ∠H(ω)\angle H(\omega)∠H(ω)

However, since we modify the phase, this means that our output signal may have a different waveform than our input.

If it is not constant, the time delay, tdt_dtd​, will vary with frequency. Meaning that our components can get misaligned and, we do not preserve the original waveform.

Filters

The idea with filters are quite intuitive, we want to remove unwanted components from a signal.

An ideal filter would be:

  • Allows for distortionless transmission of a certain interval of frequency.

  • Suppresses all remaining, unwanted frequencies.

Since, in this series we won’t design filters, we won’t get that technical.

The most usual filters are high-pass and low-pass filters. In short, a high-pass filter allows signals with a frequency higher than a certain cutoff frequency and attenuates lower frequencies.

A low-pass filter allows signals with a frequency lower than our cutoff frequency and attenuates higher frequencies.

This is in the ideal case, in real systems these cutoffs can not be instantaneous of course, they gradually filter out the frequencies.

For a physically realizable system (which means casual):

h(t)=0 ∣ t<0h(t) = 0 \ | \ t < 0h(t)=0 ∣ t<0

Sampling

Okay, now that we understand filters, let’s try to understand the approach to Nyquist-Shannon’s theorem.

In physical systems, signals are continuous-time signals, however, in our digital representations of signals we need to quantize.

We can sample the signal by taking the value, f(t)f(t)f(t) at specified instants of time.

This yields us a sequence of numbers, f(t1),f(t2),f(t3),…f(t_1), f(t_2), f(t_3), \ldotsf(t1​),f(t2​),f(t3​),… where tit_iti​ are the time instants we have sampled.

We take these samples at equal increments of time, we call this the sample period, TTT. The inverse of the sample period is the sample frequency or sampling rate denoted by Fs\mathcal{F}_sFs​.

The sampling frequency has to be sufficiently high so that we can recover our original continuous-time signal without (much) error.

Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem

A real signal whose bandwidth is limited to BBB Hz can be reconstructed exactly from its samples if the sampling frequency is Fs>2B\mathcal{F}_s > 2BFs​>2B.

Proof

Let f(t)f(t)f(t) be a band-limited signal, meaning we have a non-infinite bandwidth, in other words F(ω)=0F(\omega) = 0F(ω)=0 for ∣ω∣>ωB|\omega| > \omega_B∣ω∣>ωB​, where ωB=2πB\omega_B = 2\pi BωB​=2πB.

Let’s denote this sequence as:

fs(t)=f(t)s(t)f_s(t) = f(t) s(t)fs​(t)=f(t)s(t)
