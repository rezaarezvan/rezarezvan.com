Part 5 - Electrical fields

Date: 3/27/2023

In this part we’ll discuss electrical fields and some tools we’ll begin to use.

So far we’ve only analyzed the electrical circuits at a macro level. In this part we’ll try to dissect them at a micro-level, so let’s zoom in!

Electrical charge

To understand electrical circuits, let’s first understand electrical charge.

As we’ve seen, we use qqq, as the notation for charge. The elementary charge (the charge carried by a single proton) is:

e−=1.602⋅ 10−19Ce^{-} = 1.602 \cdot\ 10^{-19} Ce−=1.602⋅ 10−19C

We also need to remember this very important law:

The total charge within a closed system cannot change. It can only be redistributed.

Now that we’ve defined electrical charge, we can define electrical fields!

Electrical fields

Let’s start by defining what an electrical field is!

An electrical field is a force field created by electric charges.

Always remember:

  • Repulsive if charges are the same.

  • Attractive if charges are different.

Coulomb’s law

This force field that is created by the charges, we can mathematically compute these forces:

F12⃗=ke q1q20r2r12⃗\mathbf{\vec{F_{12}}} = k_{e}\ \frac{q_1 q_20}{r^2} \mathbf{\vec{r_{12}}}F12​​=ke​ r2q1​q2​0​r12​​

Where kek_{e}ke​ is Coulomb’s constant:

ke=14πε0k_e = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_{0}}ke​=4πε0​1​

Where ε0\varepsilon_{0}ε0​ is the vacuum permittivity, we’ll actually derive this later on!

ε0=10−936π\varepsilon_{0} = \frac{10^{-9}}{36\pi}ε0​=36π10−9​

Alright with all of these tools, we can finally define electrical fields with some maths!

E⃗=ke qr2r⃗\mathbf{\vec{E}} = k_{e}\ \frac{q}{r^2}\mathbf{\vec{r}}E=ke​ r2q​r

Also, given an electrical field we can calculate the force on a point of charge:

FE⃗=q E⃗\mathbf{\vec{F_{E}}} = q\ \mathbf{\vec{E}}FE​​=q E

Notice how these formulas are really similar to formulas of gravitation.

Superposition

As you could have guessed, yes, the superposition principle applies to the electrical forces and fields:

Fj⃗=∑i=1N Fij⃗E⃗=∑i=1N Ei⃗\mathbf{\vec{F_{j}}} = \sum_{i = 1}^{N}\ \mathbf{\vec{F_{ij}}} \newline \mathbf{\vec{E}} = \sum_{i = 1}^{N}\ \mathbf{\vec{E_{i}}}Fj​​=i=1∑N​ Fij​​E=i=1∑N​ Ei​​

Dipoles

Two equals and opposite charges are known as a dipole.

A dipole moment is a measure of the density of the electrical field of a dipole.

The formula

p⃗=qd⃗\mathbf{\vec{p}} = q\mathbf{\vec{d}}p​=qd

Where d⃗\mathbf{\vec{d}}d, is the direction vector, which is defined as, from negative charge to positive charge by convention.

This means, placing a dipole in an electrical field, applies a torque on the dipole:

Which means we can calculate the torque:

τ⃗=p⃗×E⃗τ=p⋅ E sin(θ)\mathbf{\vec{\tau}} = \mathbf{\vec{p}} \times \mathbf{\vec{E}} \newline \tau = p \cdot\ E\ sin(\theta)τ=p​×Eτ=p⋅ E sin(θ)

Electrical flux

So far we’ve discussed discrete number of charges and points. Imagine now instead that we have a continuous line:

So instead of:

E⃗=∑i=1N Ei⃗\mathbf{\vec{E}} = \sum_{i = 1}^{N}\ \mathbf{\vec{E_{i}}}E=i=1∑N​ Ei​​

We get:

E⃗=∫L1L2 El⃗dl\mathbf{\vec{E}} = \int_{L_{1}}^{L_{2}}\ \mathbf{\vec{E_{l}}} dlE=∫L1​L2​​ El​​dl

Now, let’s flip it around. We instead have a single point of charge and want to know how much of its electrical field passes through a line.

This is precisely electrical flux! We denote electrical flux with the Φ\PhiΦ.

Φ=∑i=1N E⃗⋅ n^\Phi = \sum_{i = 1}^{N}\ \mathbf{\vec{E}} \cdot\ \hat{n}Φ=i=1∑N​ E⋅ n^

And for the continuous case:

Φ=∫L1L2 El⃗⋅ n^⋅ dl\Phi = \int_{L_{1}}^{L_{2}}\ \mathbf{\vec{E_{l}}} \cdot\ \hat{n} \cdot\ dlΦ=∫L1​L2​​ El​​⋅ n^⋅ dl

Let’s now add another dimension, so it’s a surface

Φ=∬E⃗⋅ n^⋅ dA⃗=∬E⃗⋅ dA⃗ cos(θ)\Phi = \iint \mathbf{\vec{E}} \cdot\ \hat{n} \cdot\ d\mathbf{\vec{A}} = \iint \mathbf{\vec{E}} \cdot\ d\mathbf{\vec{A}}\ cos(\theta)Φ=∬E⋅ n^⋅ dA=∬E⋅ dA cos(θ)

If we’re instead within a closed contour:

For this we use the special integral symbol:

Φ=∯ E⃗⋅ dA⃗\Phi = \oiint\ \mathbf{\vec{E}} \cdot\ d\mathbf{\vec{A}}Φ=∬​ E⋅ dA

What is the total flux through this surface (circle).

E⃗=q4πr2ε0A=4πr2\mathbf{\vec{E}} = \frac{q}{4\pi r^2 \varepsilon_{0}} \newline A = 4\pi r^2E=4πr2ε0​q​A=4πr2

Therefore:

Φ=q4πr2ε0 4πr2\Phi = \frac{q}{4\pi r^2 \varepsilon_{0}}\ 4\pi r^2 \newlineΦ=4πr2ε0​q​ 4πr2 Φ=qε0\Phi = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_{0}}Φ=ε0​q​

Gauss’s law

This is Gauss’s law!

ΦE=∯E⃗⋅ dA⃗=qε0\Phi_{E} = \oiint \mathbf{\vec{E}} \cdot\ d\mathbf{\vec{A}} = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_{0}}ΦE​=∬​E⋅ dA=ε0​q​
