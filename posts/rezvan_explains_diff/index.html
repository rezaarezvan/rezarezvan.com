Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE)

The Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE) consists of two models (realized by (deep) neural networks).

The first model is the encoder and the second model is the decoder. The input vector (typically an image) is denoted by $\mathbf{x}$, and the output vector (also typically an imagehatmathbfx) is denoted by $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$.

The intermediate state/vector is called the latent variable

Figure 1: VAE Architecture 1

The job of the encoder is to extract a meaningful representation for $\mathbf{x}$, wheras the job of the decoder is to generate a new image from the latent variable $\mathbf{z}$.

The latent variable $\mathbf{z}$ has two special roles in the VAE:

  1. Encapsulate information that can be used to describe $\mathbf{x}$.
    • This encoding could be lossy but the main objective is to preserve as much information of $\mathbf{x}$ as possible.
  2. The latent variable serves as a “seed” from which the output $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ is generated.
    • Two different $\mathbf{z}$‘s should (in theory) generate two different $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$‘s.

Definition 1.1 Latent Variable:

In a probabilistic model, latent variables $\mathbf{z}$ are variables that we do not observe and hence are not part of the training dataset, although they are port of the model.

The variational in VAE is from calculus of variations, i.e., studies the optimization problems over functions. We are trying to search for the optimal probability distributions that describe $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{z}$.

Consider the following distributions,

  • $p(\mathbf{x})$: The true distribution of our data, we don’t (and never will) know this. The objective of generative models is find ways to draw samples from $p(\mathbf{x})$ (approximations). If we knew $p(\mathbf{x})$ we could just draw sample $\mathbf{x}$ that maximizes $\log p(\mathbf{x})$.
  • $p(\mathbf{z})$: The distribution of latent variable. Typically, we will model it as a zero-mean unit-variance (multivariate) Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$. The reason is that linear transformations of Gaussians remain Gaussian. Secondly, any distribution can be generated by mapping a Gaussian through a sufficiently complex function 2.
  • $p(\mathbf{z} | \mathbf{x})$: The conditional distribution associated with the encoder. Same as $p(\mathbf{x})$, we don’t know this distribution. $p(\mathbf{z} | \mathbf{x})$ itself is not the encoder, but **the encoder has to do something so that it will behave consistently with $p(\mathbf{z} | \mathbf{x})$.
  • $p(\mathbf{x} | \mathbf{z})$: The conditional distribution associated with the decoder. Again, we don’t know this distribution.

When we switch from classical parametric models to deep neural networks, our notation of latent variables changes.

Definition 1.2 Deep Latent Variable Model:

Deep Latent Variables are latent variables whose distribution $p(\mathbf{z})$, $p(\mathbf{x} | \mathbf{z})$, or $p(\mathbf{z} | \mathbf{x})$ are parameterized by deep neural networks.

Flow Matching

Let $p_t(x_t)$ be the target (true) distribution, $x_t = \psi_t(x)$ is the flow function with the following property,

$$ \frac{d}{dt} \psi_t(x) = u_t(\psi_t(x)), $$

where $u_t(x_t)$ is the true (unkown) vector field.

Therefore, we want to learn $v_t(x_t)$ to approximate $u_t(x_t)$, therefore our objective (that we want to minimize) is,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) - u_t(x_t) \Vert^2 \right]. $$

However, since we do not have $p_t(x_t)$ or $u_t(x_t)$, we can’t compute the expectation above.

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) \Vert^2 - 2 v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t) + \Vert u_t(x_t) \Vert^2 \right] \newline $$

We know that $\mathbb{E}_{p(x)} = \int p(x) dx$, so we can rewrite the middle term as,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)} \left[2 v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t) \right] = 2 \int v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t) \cdot p_t(x_t) dx_t. $$

By marginalizing $u_t(x_t)$,

$$ u_t(x_t) = \int u_t(x_t | x_1) \frac{p_t(x_t | x_1) q(x_1)}{p_t(x_t)} dx_1. $$

Thus,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)} \left[2 v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t) \right] = 2 \int v_t(x_t) \cdot \frac{\int u_t(x_t | x_1) \frac{p_t(x_t | x_1) q(x_1)}{p_t(x_t)} dx_1}{p_t(x_t)} p_t(x_t) dx_t. $$

By Fubini’s Theorem,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)} \left[2 v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t) \right] = 2 \int \int v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t | x_1) p_t(x_t | x_1) q(x_1) dx_1 dx_t. $$

Written as an expectation,

$$ 2 \mathbb{E}_{x_1 \sim p_t(x_t | x_1), x_1 \sim q(x_1)} \left[v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t | x_1) \right]. $$

Plugging this back into our objective,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t | x_1), x_1 \sim q(x_1)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) \Vert^2 - 2 v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t | x_1) + \Vert u_t(x_t) \Vert^2 \right] \newline $$

Now, let’s do the classic $+c - c$ trick,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t | x_1), x_1 \sim q(x_1)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) \Vert^2 - 2 v_t(x_t) \cdot u_t(x_t | x_1) + \Vert u_t(x_t) \Vert^2 + \Vert u_t(x_t | x_1) \Vert^2 - \Vert u_t(x_t | x_1) \Vert^2 \right] \newline $$

Now, we can rewrite this, using our algebraic identity, as our first step,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t | x_1), x_1 \sim q(x_1)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) - u_t(x_t | x_1) \Vert^2 + \Vert u_t(x_t) \Vert^2 - \Vert u_t(x_t | x_1) \Vert^2 \right] \newline $$

Since expectation is a linear operator, we can split our expectation,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t | x_1), x_1 \sim q(x_1)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) - u_t(x_t | x_1) \Vert^2 \right] + \ldots $$

The other terms are constants (since we are training $v_t$), so we can ignore them (when maximizing the objective).

What we started with, $\mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) - u_t(x_t) \Vert^2 \right]$, is the flow matching objective.

What we ended up with, $\mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t | x_1), x_1 \sim q(x_1)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) - u_t(x_t | x_1) \Vert^2 \right]$, is the conditional flow matching objective.

Recall that the flow function is defined as,

$$ \frac{d}{dt} \psi_t(x) = u_t(\psi_t(x)) $$

Thus, the conditional flow function is defined as,

$$ \frac{d}{dt} \psi_t(x | x_1) = u_t(\psi_t(x) | x_1) $$

Let’s now define $\psi_t(x)$ as,

$$ \psi_t(x) = \sigma_t(x_1) x + \mu_t(x_1) $$

If $x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, which we denote as $x_0$, then $psi_t(x_0)$ is the canonical transformation of the flow,

$$ \psi_t(x_0) = \sigma_t(x_1) x_0 + \mu_t(x_1) $$

This is the simplest form of interpolation of two distributions, only allowing shifts and scaling.

If we define $\sigma_t(x_1) = 1 - t$ and $\mu_t(x_1) = tx_1$, then we can interpolate between two distributions,

$$ \psi_t(x_0) = (1 - t) x_0 + t x_1. $$

Thus, we can rewrite our objective as,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t | x_1), x_1 \sim q(x_1)} \left[ \Vert v_t(x_t) - \frac{d}{dt} \psi_t(x_0) \Vert^2 \right] $$

We can reparemeterize this as,

$$ \mathbb{E}_{x_t \sim p_t(x_t | x_1), x_1 \sim q(x_1)} \left[ \Vert v_t(\psi_t(x_0)) - \frac{d}{dt} \psi_t(x_0) \Vert^2 \right] $$

The derivative of $\psi_t(x_0)$ is,

$$ \frac{d}{dt} \psi_t(x_0) = x_1 - x_0 $$

So at training we,

  1. Sample noise from a noise distribution — $x_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.
  2. Sample a data point from our dataset — $x_1 \sim \mathcal{D}$.
  3. Sample a $t \sim \mathcal{U}(0, 1)$.
  4. Then we calculate $x_t$ by linearly interpolating between $x_0$ and $x_1$ — $x_t = (1 - t) x_0 + t x_1$.
  5. (4) is our input to our neural network $v_t$.
  6. (5) tries to predict the vector from $x_0$ to $x_1$.

Score-Based Diffusion on 2D Gaussians

Okay I’ve thought a bit more about the problem and I initially thought about this as well, but I think this is precisely what score-based (and to some extent flow matching) diffusion does.

I can start with showing how this solves our toy problem (and is generalizable),

A standard 2D (multivariate) Gaussian is defined as, $$ p(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2\pi \sqrt{|\Sigma|}} \exp \left( -\frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{x} - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \mu) \right) $$

Let our linear transformation be $T(\mathbf{x}) = A \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}$.

We know that our transformed Gaussian is also Gaussian, but with mean $A \mu + \mathbf{b}$ and covariance $A \Sigma A^T$.

Now consider the log-density of the Gaussian,

$$ \log p(\mathbf{x}) = -\frac{1}{2} \log (\mathbf{x} - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \mu) - \frac{1}{2} \log ((2\pi)^2 |\Sigma|) $$

Then the gradient (w.r.t. $\mathbf{x}$) of the log-density is,

$$ \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p(\mathbf{x}) = -\Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \mu) $$

Which is a linear function of $\mathbf{x}$,

$$ \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p(\mathbf{x}) = -\Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{x} + \Sigma^{-1} \mu $$

So, so long as our model is a differentiable, that approximates $p(\mathbf{x})$, we can compute $\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p(\mathbf{x})$.

So, we know that,

$$ \mathbb{E}[\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p(\mathbf{x})] = M \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{c} = -\Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{x} + \Sigma^{-1} \mu $$

Now, score-based diffusion sounds fancy, but score is just $s(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p(\mathbf{x})$.

So, for Gaussians we know this is linear, but we can model it to be more complicated.

The idea (that I understand anyway) is that, if we knew $s(\mathbf{x})$ for every $\mathbf{x}$, we could recover $p(\mathbf{x})$,

$$ \log p(\mathbf{x}) = \int s(\mathbf{z}) d\mathbf{z} + C $$

Essentially, the idea is to pick a path in $\mathbb{R}^N$ and integrate the score (log-density gradient) along that path.

From what I understand for complex distributions, one can do local fits (i.e., fit a “local” Gaussian).
