Why 3D Molecular Generation Matters

Generating 3D molecular structures is fundamentally more challenging than 2D graph generation because:

  1. The distribution must respect physical constraints
  2. The representation must be $E(3)$-invariant (same molecule regardless of rotation/translation)
  3. Valid molecules require matching both topological and geometrical properties

Previous approaches like diffusion models (MiDi, EQGAT-diff) have achieved impressive results but require hundreds or thousands of model evaluations, making them impractical for real-world drug discovery workflows.

The Semla Architecture

At the heart of SemlaFlow is the Semla architecture that introduces a approach to equivariant message passing. Instead of computing full pairwise messages in the original feature space (which scales quadratically with feature dimension), Semla projects features into a lower-dimensional latent space,

$$ \begin{align} \begin{split} \mathbf{m_{i,j}}^{(\text{inv})}, \mathbf{m_{i,j}}^{(\text{equi})} & = \Omega_\theta(\mathbf{\tilde{h_i}}, \mathbf{\tilde{h_j}}, \mathbf{\tilde{x_i}} \cdot \mathbf{\tilde{x_j}}), \newline \mathbf{\tilde{h_k}} & = \mathbf{W_{\theta}}^3 \phi_{\text{inv}} (\mathbf{h_k}^{ff}), \newline \mathbf{\tilde{x_k}} & = \phi_{\text{equi}} (\mathbf{x}_k^{ff}). \end{split} \end{align} $$

This approach is brilliantly simple yet effective. By computing attention in a compressed latent space (with dimension $d_l \ll d_{\text{inv}}$), Semla achieves 3-5√ó faster inference while maintaining or improving generative quality.

Flow Matching: The Perfect Training Framework

SemlaFlow employs conditional flow matching 1, which learns a vector field to transport noise to data samples. SemlaFlow uses this framework to align noise vectors with the data manifold, improving sample quality and reducing training time. The final loss function is a combination of MSE and cross-entropy losses,

$$ \mathcal{L_{\text{SemlaFlow}}} = \lambda_x \mathcal{L_{\text{MSE}}} (\tilde{x_1}, x_1) + \lambda_a \mathcal{L_{\text{CE}}} (\tilde{a_1}, a_1) + \lambda_b \mathcal{L_{\text{CE}}} (\tilde{b_1}, b_1) + \lambda_c \mathcal{L_{\text{CE}}} (\tilde{c_1}, c_1), $$

where $\mathbf{\tilde{x}_1}, \mathbf{\tilde{a}_1}, \mathbf{\tilde{b}_1}, \mathbf{\tilde{c}_1}$ are the predicted coordinates, atom types, bond types, and charges, respectively.

The approach jointly generates coordinates, atom types, bond types, and charges in a single model. Combined with equivariant optimal transport 2 for noise alignment, this results in remarkably efficient sampling.

By measuring:

  1. Energy per atom (overall molecular energy)
  2. Strain energy (difference between generated and optimized conformations)

We get a much more realistic assessment of chemical quality. SemlaFlow outperforms previous methods on these metrics, demonstrating its ability to generate not just valid but physically realistic structures.
